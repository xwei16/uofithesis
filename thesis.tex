\abstract{
    % This is the abstract of the thesis. It should be a concise summary of the
    % research, including the main findings and conclusions. The abstract should
    % be no more than 350 words in length.

    Machine-learning-based Adaptive Bitrate (ABR) controllers, such as
    Pensieve~\cite{pensieve}, have demonstrated substantial Quality of
    Experience (QoE) improvements over hand-crafted heuristics in video
    streaming systems. Yet their black-box neural nature introduces two
    intertwined problems: vulnerability to adversarial manipulation and the
    absence of formal correctness guarantees. This thesis addresses both
    through a three-part framework progressing from exposing vulnerabilities,
    to certifying the environment, to formally verifying closed-loop QoE
    properties.

    We begin by demonstrating that Pensieve is highly susceptible to
    specification-guided adversarial attacks. Using pre- and post-condition
    specifications mined by \texttt{SpecTRA}~\cite{spec-tra}, we construct a
    Projected Gradient Descent (PGD) attack that perturbs only the most
    recent chunk download time by at most $10\%$. This lightweight manipulation reduces selected bitrate by $35$--$65\%$
    and degrades single-round QoE by $30$--$40\%$ across $581$ successful
    attacks out of $30{,}000$ test states, while remaining within
    empirically valid network bounds.

    These vulnerabilities motivate the need for formal guarantees, which
    in turn require a trustworthy model of the environment in which the
    controller operates. We address this by constructing a formally certified
    neural predictor $f^{\ast}$ for the ABR environment transition function
    $f$. Trained on real-world traces from the Puffer dataset using $19$
    system features, our best model achieves a Clopper--Pearson certified
    lower bound of $0.9846$ at $90\%$ confidence---at least $98.46\%$ of
    predictions incur less than $10\%$ normalised error---closing the
    critical gap between neural controller analysis and the environment
    dynamics it must reason about.

    Building on this certified predictor, we design an end-to-end QoE
    verification pipeline that reasons about system behaviour across
    multiple control rounds. Composing $f^{\ast}$ with 
    \texttt{auto\_LiRPA}~\cite{auto-lirpa} bound propagation. A parallel
    branch-and-bound search partitions the input perturbation set into safe
    regions---subsets of states for which Pensieve is certified to select a
    specific action---then propagates interval bounds through $f^{\ast}$ to
    obtain guaranteed next-state bounds. Iterating over multiple control
    rounds builds a hierarchical verification tree supporting formal
    reasoning about multi-step QoE properties of the complete
    neuro-symbolic control loop. 
    We further establish theoretical soundness guarantees for
    the pipeline and derive a statistical compounding bound characterising
    how environment prediction error accumulates across rounds.
 
    Together, these contributions establish a principled methodology for
    advancing neural systems controllers from empirical evaluation to formal
    safety certification.


}

\section{Introduction}

    Online video accounts for the dominant share of global Internet
    traffic~\cite{cloudinary-quality}. To deliver smooth playback over volatile
    network links, nearly all modern streaming players employ an
    \emph{adaptive bitrate} (ABR) controller: a component that selects the
    video quality level for each chunk based on current network conditions,
    balancing video quality, rebuffering risk, and quality-switch penalties
    into a single Quality of Experience (QoE) objective~\cite{pensieve}.
    
    For decades, ABR controllers were built from hand-crafted heuristics.
    Rule-based algorithms such as BOLA~\cite{bola} and
    MPC~\cite{mpc} are simple and interpretable, but leave
    substantial QoE improvements unrealised because they cannot capture the
    complex, nonlinear interactions among bandwidth variability, buffer
    dynamics, and chunk sizes. Pensieve~\cite{pensieve} addresses
    this gap with a neural network controller trained via reinforcement
    learning (RL) to maximise QoE-related rewards directly, achieving gains
    of $10$--$70\%$ over rule-based baselines. Other ML-based approaches,
    such as Fugu~\cite{fugu}, combine a supervised learned throughput
    predictor with model-predictive control to similar effect.
    
    This performance advantage comes with a fundamental cost. Neural
    networks are opaque: their decision boundaries are encoded in millions
    of floating-point parameters, making their behaviour under worst-case
    inputs difficult to reason about or formally verify. A decade of
    research in computer vision has shown that small, carefully crafted
    perturbations can cause neural networks to produce confident but
    incorrect outputs~\cite{attack}. This thesis
    demonstrates that the same fragility afflicts neural systems
    controllers, and then develops the formal tools needed to reason about
    it.

    \subsection{Problem Statement and Challenges}
    
    Extending formal analysis to neural ABR controllers raises challenges
    that go beyond classical neural network verification. First, ABR lacks a
    universal definition of correctness: different algorithms disagree under
    identical conditions, and no single objective is accepted across
    deployments. We address this by using
    SpecTRA~\cite{spec-tra} to mine pre-/post-condition
    specifications directly from Pensieve's training traces, grounding
    correctness in observed behaviour. Second, Pensieve's input features
    are physically coupled---throughput is definitionally chunk size divided
    by download time---so perturbations must respect these dependencies to
    remain physically realisable. Third, and most critically, Pensieve
    operates in a \emph{closed loop}: each bitrate decision shapes the
    future network state the controller will observe. The system evolves as
    \begin{equation}
    s_{t+1} = f(s_t,\, a_t), \qquad a_t = \mathrm{NN}(s_t),
    \label{eq:closed-loop}
    \end{equation}
    where $\mathrm{NN}$ is the neural controller and $f$ is the \emph{environment
    transition function}. While $\mathrm{NN}$ can be analysed with existing
    verification tools, $f$ encodes stochastic network dynamics whose exact
    form is unknown. Any formal guarantee about the \emph{system} requires
    a trustworthy, bounded model of $f$---a gap that prior work on NN4Sys
    verification has left unaddressed.

    \subsection{Contributions}
    
    This thesis makes three contributions that together form an end-to-end
    framework for formally reasoning about Pensieve's closed-loop QoE
    behaviour.
    
    \paragraph{Adversarial vulnerability analysis~(Chapter~\ref{chap:attack}).}
    We present the first systematic study of adversarial attacks on ML-based
    ABR controllers. Using SpecTRA-derived specifications to constrain
    perturbations to physically valid states, we construct a PGD attack that
    increases the most recent download time by at most $10\%$. This
    manipulation reduces selected bitrate by $35$--$65\%$ and degrades
    single-round QoE by $30$--$40\%$ across $581$ successful attacks out of
    $30{,}000$ test states, while remaining undetectable by coarse anomaly
    monitors. These results establish a concrete threat model and motivate
    the need for principled formal defences.
    
    \paragraph{Statistically certified environment model~(Chapter~\ref{chap:env}).}
    We construct a formally bounded neural predictor $f^{\ast}$ for the
    environment transition function $f$, trained on real-world traces from
    the Puffer dataset~\cite{fugu}. Applying Clopper--Pearson
    confidence intervals~\cite{clopper-pearson} to the empirical error
    distribution, our best model achieves a certified lower bound of
    $0.9846$ at $90\%$ confidence: at least $98.46\%$ of predictions incur
    less than $10\%$ normalised error. This certified predictor provides the
    trustworthy symbolic component that closed-loop verification requires.
    
    \paragraph{End-to-end QoE verification pipeline~(Chapter~\ref{chap:verify}).}
    We design and implement a multi-round verification pipeline that
    alternates between a Pensieve phase and an environment phase.
    In the Pensieve phase, auto\_LiRPA~\cite{auto-lirpa} with
    CROWN-Optimised bound propagation and a parallel branch-and-bound search
    partition the input region into safe subregions---subsets for which one
    action provably dominates all others. In the environment phase, the
    certified predictor $f^{\ast}$ propagates interval bounds forward to
    obtain guaranteed next-state bounds. Iterating for up to five control
    rounds builds a hierarchical verification tree supporting formal
    reasoning about multi-step QoE properties. We further establish
    theoretical soundness guarantees and a statistical compounding bound
    quantifying how prediction error accumulates across rounds.

\section{Background}

    \label{chap:background}
    
    This chapter reviews the technical foundations on which the three
    contributions of this thesis rest: the ABR streaming pipeline and QoE
    objective, the Pensieve neural controller, the SpecTRA specification
    mining, neural network verification via LiRPA and branch-and-bound
    search, and statistical certification via Clopper--Pearson intervals.
    
    \section{Adaptive Bitrate Video Streaming}
    \label{sec:bg-abr}
    
    Video content is delivered in discrete \emph{chunks}, each typically
    $2$--$4$ seconds of playback. After each download, the ABR controller
    selects the quality level---and hence the file size and bandwidth
    demand---of the next chunk. The controller optimises QoE, balancing
    bitrate, rebuffering time, and quality-switch penalties:
    \begin{equation}
    \qoe = \textstyle\sum R_t \;-\; \lambda_1 \sum T^{\mathrm{rebuf}}_t
            \;-\; \lambda_2 \sum |R_t - R_{t-1}|,
    \label{eq:qoe}
    \end{equation}
    where $R_t$ is the bitrate of chunk $t$, $T^{\mathrm{rebuf}}_t$ is
    any rebuffering time incurred, and $\lambda_1, \lambda_2$ are penalty
    weights. Higher bitrates improve visual quality but increase download
    time and rebuffering risk; frequent quality switches are perceptually
    jarring. Rule-based controllers such as BOLA~\cite{bola} and
    MPC~\cite{mpc} encode expert heuristics to manage this
    trade-off, but their fixed rules cannot adapt to the full complexity of
    real network dynamics.
    
    \subsection{The Pensieve Controller}
    \label{sec:bg-pensieve}
    
    Pensieve~\cite{pensieve} is a neural network controller
    trained via reinforcement learning to maximise the QoE reward
    in~\eqref{eq:qoe}. At each decision step, the player constructs a $6
    \times 8$ state matrix $S$ whose rows encode: (1) past throughput
    samples, (2) past download times, (3) post-download buffer occupancy
    levels, (4) past selected bitrates, (5) available chunk sizes at all
    quality levels, and (6) the number of remaining chunks. This matrix is
    flattened into a $48$-dimensional vector and fed into a two-layer fully
    connected actor network. The network outputs a softmax distribution over
    six discrete bitrate actions $a \in \{300, 750, 1200, 1850, 2850,
    4300\}$~kbps. The actor is trained with a reward function $R =
    R_{\mathrm{bitrate}} - 4.3 \cdot R_{\mathrm{rebuffer}} -
    R_{\mathrm{switch}}$, achieving QoE improvements of $10$--$70\%$ over
    traditional controllers. Importantly, at deployment time Pensieve
    operates purely as a feed-forward neural network: the RL training
    procedure is offline, and the deployed artefact is a fixed neural
    policy amenable to standard verification tools.
    
    \subsection{Specification Mining with SpecTRA}
    \label{sec:bg-spectra}
    
    SpecTRA~\cite{spec-tra} automatically generates
    pre-/post-condition specifications for neural network controllers by
    analysing their training traces. Given logged input--output pairs from
    Pensieve's training data, SpecTRA clusters the trace into
    operating regimes and extracts per-feature interval bounds (pre-conditions)
    together with the associated output constraints (post-conditions). These
    specifications serve two roles in this thesis: they constrain
    adversarial perturbations to physically plausible states
    (Chapter~\ref{chap:attack}), and they provide the initial perturbation
    sets from which the verification pipeline launches
    (Chapter~\ref{chap:verify}).
    
    \subsection{Neural Network Verification: LiRPA and Branch-and-Bound}
    \label{sec:bg-lirpa}
    
    \emph{Linear Relaxation-based Perturbation Analysis} (LiRPA) is a
    family of methods for computing certified bounds on neural network
    outputs over bounded input sets. Given an input region $[\ell, u]$,
    LiRPA methods propagate linear relaxations of each layer's activation
    function forward or backward through the network, producing
    over-approximations $[\underline{y}, \overline{y}]$ of the true output
    range. The \texttt{auto\_LiRPA} library~\cite{auto-lirpa}
    implements several variants: Interval Bound Propagation (IBP), which is
    fast but loose, and CROWN~\cite{abcrown} and its optimised
    variant CROWN-Opt, which are tighter but more expensive. All variants
    are \emph{sound}: the computed bounds provably contain the true output
    for all inputs in the region.
    
    Branch-and-bound (BaB) search~\cite{bunel2018bab} improves bound
    tightness by recursively splitting the input region along dimensions
    where the relaxation is loose, applying LiRPA to each sub-region, and
    pruning sub-regions that are already certified. In this thesis, BaB is
    used to partition the input perturbation set into \emph{safe regions}
    in which a single \pensieve{} action is provably dominant.
    
    \section{Statistical Certification: Clopper--Pearson Intervals}
    \label{sec:bg-cp}
    
    The Clopper--Pearson method~\cite{clopper1934use} provides exact
    binomial confidence intervals without parametric assumptions. Given $n$
    i.i.d.\ test samples of which $k$ satisfy a success criterion, it
    models each prediction as a Bernoulli trial with unknown success
    probability $p$ and computes:
    \[
    \mathrm{CI}_{1-\alpha}(p) =
    \left[
        \mathrm{Beta}^{-1}\!\left(\tfrac{\alpha}{2};\, k,\, n-k+1\right),\;
        \mathrm{Beta}^{-1}\!\left(1 - \tfrac{\alpha}{2};\, k+1,\, n-k\right)
    \right],
    \]
    where $\alpha$ is the significance level. The lower bound of this
    interval gives a conservative but rigorous guarantee on the true
    success probability. In Chapter~\ref{chap:env}, we apply this method
    with $\alpha = 0.10$ to certify the fraction of inputs for which
    $\fstar$ predicts within $10\%$ normalised error of the true
    environment output.
