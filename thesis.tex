\abstract{
    % This is the abstract of the thesis. It should be a concise summary of the
    % research, including the main findings and conclusions. The abstract should
    % be no more than 350 words in length.

    Machine-learning-based Adaptive Bitrate (ABR) controllers, such as
    Pensieve~\cite{pensieve}, have demonstrated substantial Quality of
    Experience (QoE) improvements over hand-crafted heuristics in video
    streaming systems.  Yet their black-box neural nature introduces two
    intertwined problems: vulnerability to adversarial manipulation and
    the absence of formal correctness guarantees.  This thesis addresses
    both through a three-part framework that progresses from exposing
    vulnerabilities, to certifying the operating environment, to formally
    verifying closed-loop QoE properties.
 
    We first show that Pensieve is highly susceptible to
    specification-guided adversarial attacks.  Using pre- and
    post-condition specifications mined by
    \texttt{SpecTRA}~\cite{spec-tra}, we construct a Projected Gradient
    Descent (PGD) attack that perturbs only the most recent chunk
    download time by at most $10\%$.  This lightweight manipulation
    reduces the selected bitrate by $35$--$65\%$ and degrades
    single-round QoE by $30$--$40\%$ across $581$ successful attacks out
    of $30{,}000$ test states, while remaining within empirically valid
    network bounds.
 
    These vulnerabilities motivate the need for formal guarantees, which
    in turn require a trustworthy model of the environment in which the
    controller operates.  We address this by training a neural predictor
    $f^{\ast}$ for the ABR environment transition function~$f$ on
    real-world traces from the Puffer dataset using $19$ system features.
    Applying Clopper--Pearson confidence
    intervals~\cite{clopper-pearson}, our best model achieves a certified
    lower bound of $0.9846$ at $90\%$ confidence---at least $98.46\%$ of
    predictions incur less than $10\%$ normalised error---filling a
    critical gap in neural controller verification: a trustworthy model
    of the environment dynamics.
 
    Building on this certified predictor, we design an end-to-end QoE
    verification pipeline that reasons about system behaviour across
    multiple control rounds.  The pipeline alternates between a
    \emph{Pensieve phase}, in which
    \texttt{auto\_LiRPA}~\cite{auto-lirpa} bound propagation and
    parallel branch-and-bound search partition the input region into safe
    subregions where a single action provably dominates, and an
    \emph{environment phase}, in which $f^{\ast}$ propagates interval
    bounds forward to obtain guaranteed next-state bounds.  Iterating
    over multiple rounds builds a hierarchical verification tree
    supporting formal reasoning about multi-step QoE properties of the
    complete neuro-symbolic control loop.  We further establish
    theoretical soundness guarantees and derive a statistical compounding
    bound characterising how environment prediction error accumulates
    across rounds.
 
    Together, these contributions establish a principled methodology for
    advancing neural systems controllers from empirical evaluation to
    formal safety certification.


}

\section{Introduction}

    \label{chap:intro}
 
    Online video accounts for the dominant share of global Internet
    traffic~\cite{cloudinary-quality}.  To deliver smooth playback over
    volatile network links, nearly all modern streaming players employ an
    \emph{adaptive bitrate} (ABR) controller: a component that selects
    the video quality level for each chunk based on current network
    conditions, balancing video quality, rebuffering risk, and
    quality-switch penalties into a single Quality of Experience (QoE)
    objective~\cite{pensieve}.
 
    For decades, ABR controllers were built from hand-crafted heuristics.
    Rule-based algorithms such as BOLA~\cite{bola} and
    MPC~\cite{mpc} are simple and interpretable, but they leave
    substantial QoE improvements unrealised because they cannot capture
    the complex, nonlinear interactions among bandwidth variability,
    buffer dynamics, and chunk sizes.  Pensieve~\cite{pensieve} addresses
    this gap with a neural network controller trained via reinforcement
    learning (RL) to maximise QoE-related rewards directly, achieving
    gains of $10$--$70\%$ over rule-based baselines.  Other ML-based
    approaches, such as Fugu~\cite{fugu}, combine a supervised throughput
    predictor with model-predictive control to similar effect.
 
    This performance advantage comes at a fundamental cost.  Neural
    networks are opaque: their decision boundaries are encoded in
    millions of floating-point parameters, making their behaviour under
    worst-case inputs difficult to reason about or formally verify.  A
    decade of research in computer vision has shown that small, carefully
    crafted perturbations can cause neural networks to produce confident
    but incorrect outputs~\cite{attack}.  This thesis demonstrates that
    the same fragility afflicts neural systems controllers, and then
    develops the formal tools needed to reason about it.
 
 
    % ---- 1.1  Problem Statement and Challenges ----------------------------
    \subsection{Problem Statement and Challenges}
    \label{subsec:problem}
 
    The overarching goal of this thesis is to develop a principled
    framework for formally reasoning about the closed-loop QoE behaviour
    of neural ABR controllers.  Achieving this goal requires overcoming
    three challenges that go beyond classical neural network
    verification.
 
    \paragraph{Challenge~1: Absence of a correctness specification.}
    ABR streaming lacks a universal definition of correctness: different
    algorithms disagree under identical conditions, and no single QoE
    objective is accepted across deployments.  We address this by using
    SpecTRA~\cite{spec-tra} to mine pre-/post-condition specifications
    directly from Pensieve's training traces, grounding correctness in
    observed behaviour rather than an externally imposed standard.
 
    \paragraph{Challenge~2: Physical coupling of input features.}
    Pensieve's input features are not independent.  Throughput, for
    example, is definitionally chunk size divided by download time.
    Perturbations applied to one feature must respect these physical
    dependencies to remain realisable; otherwise, adversarial analyses
    and verification results describe states that can never arise in
    practice.  Both our attack construction
    (Chapter~\ref{chap:attack}) and our verification pipeline
    (Chapter~\ref{chap:verify}) enforce such consistency constraints.
 
    \paragraph{Challenge~3: Closed-loop environment dynamics.}
    Most critically, Pensieve operates in a \emph{closed loop}: each
    bitrate decision shapes the future network state the controller will
    observe.  The system evolves as
    \begin{equation}
        s_{t+1} = f(s_t,\, a_t), \qquad a_t = \mathrm{NN}(s_t),
        \label{eq:closed-loop}
    \end{equation}
    where $\mathrm{NN}$ is the neural controller and $f$ is the
    \emph{environment transition function}.  While $\mathrm{NN}$ can be
    analysed with existing verification tools, $f$ encodes stochastic
    network dynamics whose exact form is unknown.  Any formal guarantee
    about the \emph{system}---rather than the controller in
    isolation---requires a trustworthy, bounded model of $f$, a gap that
    prior work on neural-network-based systems (NN4Sys) verification has
    left unaddressed.
 
 
    % ---- 1.2  Contributions -----------------------------------------------
    \subsection{Contributions}
    \label{subsec:contributions}
 
    This thesis makes three contributions that together form an
    end-to-end framework for formally reasoning about Pensieve's
    closed-loop QoE behaviour.
 
    \paragraph{Adversarial vulnerability analysis
               (Chapter~\ref{chap:attack}).}
    We present the first systematic study of adversarial attacks on
    ML-based ABR controllers.  Using SpecTRA-derived specifications to
    constrain perturbations to physically valid states, we construct a
    PGD attack that increases the most recent download time by at most
    $10\%$.  Across $30{,}000$ test states, the attack finds $581$
    successful adversarial examples that reduce the selected bitrate by
    $35$--$65\%$ and degrade single-round QoE by $30$--$40\%$, while
    remaining within empirically valid network bounds and undetectable by
    coarse anomaly monitors.  These results establish a concrete threat
    model and motivate the need for principled formal defences.
 
    \paragraph{Statistically certified environment model
               (Chapter~\ref{chap:env}).}
    We construct a formally bounded neural predictor $f^{\ast}$ for the
    environment transition function~$f$, trained on real-world traces
    from the Puffer dataset~\cite{fugu}.  Applying Clopper--Pearson
    confidence intervals~\cite{clopper-pearson} to the empirical error
    distribution, our best model achieves a certified lower bound of
    $0.9846$ at $90\%$ confidence: at least $98.46\%$ of predictions
    incur less than $10\%$ normalised error.  This certified predictor
    fills the missing symbolic component that closed-loop verification
    requires, bridging the gap between neural controller analysis and
    the real-world environment dynamics it must account for.
 
    \paragraph{End-to-end QoE verification pipeline
               (Chapter~\ref{chap:verify}).}
    We design and implement a multi-round verification pipeline that
    alternates between two phases.  In the \emph{Pensieve phase},
    \texttt{auto\_LiRPA}~\cite{auto-lirpa} with CROWN-Optimised bound
    propagation and a parallel branch-and-bound search partition the
    input region into \emph{safe subregions}---subsets of the state space
    for which one action provably dominates all others.  In the
    \emph{environment phase}, the certified predictor $f^{\ast}$
    propagates interval bounds forward to obtain guaranteed next-state
    bounds.  Iterating for up to five control rounds builds a
    hierarchical verification tree supporting formal reasoning about
    multi-step QoE properties of the complete neuro-symbolic control
    loop.  We further establish theoretical soundness guarantees and a
    statistical compounding bound quantifying how prediction error
    accumulates across rounds.

    \subsection{Video Streaming and the Pensieve Controller}
    \label{subsec:streaming}
    
    Online video has become the dominant load on the public Internet,
    accounting for more than 82\% of consumer IP traffic according to
    Cisco's Visual Networking Index~\cite{cloudinary-quality}.
    To keep playback smooth over volatile links, nearly all modern streaming
    players employ an ABR controller.
    Video content is delivered in discrete \emph{chunks}, each typically
    $2$--$4$~seconds of playback.
    After downloading each chunk, the client uses recent network
    metrics---throughput and buffer occupancy, among others---to select the
    quality level of the next chunk.
    
    Conventional ABR schemes such as \emph{BOLA}~\cite{bola} and
    \emph{MPC}~\cite{mpc} encode expert-designed rules; for example,
    ``maintain at least $B$~seconds of buffered video, then select the
    highest bitrate whose predicted download time is less than $B - C$,''
    where $B$ and $C$ are hand-tuned constants.
    While these heuristics perform reasonably under typical conditions, they
    struggle to capture the complex, nonlinear relationships among bandwidth
    variability, buffer dynamics, and chunk size.
    
    The primary objective of an ABR controller is to maximise QoE, which
    balances three factors: video quality (higher bitrates improve visual
    fidelity but increase download time and rebuffering risk), rebuffering
    (playback stalls severely degrade user experience), and quality switches
    (frequent bitrate changes are perceptually jarring).
    A standard QoE metric combines these factors as
    \begin{equation}
        \text{QoE}
            = \sum_t \text{bitrate}_t
            - \lambda_1 \cdot \sum_t \text{rebuffer time}_t
            - \lambda_2 \cdot \sum_t \lvert\Delta\text{bitrate}_t\rvert,
        \label{eq:qoe}
    \end{equation}
    where $\lambda_1$ and $\lambda_2$ are penalty weights that encode
    deployment-specific preferences.
    
    \paragraph{Pensieve.}
    Pensieve~\cite{pensieve} replaces hand-crafted heuristics with a neural
    policy trained via RL.
    At each decision point the player constructs a $6 \times 8$ state matrix
    $\mathbf{S}$ whose rows capture:
    (i)~past throughput samples,
    (ii)~past download times,
    (iii)~past buffer occupancy levels,
    (iv)~past selected bitrates,
    (v)~available chunk sizes at all quality levels, and
    (vi)~the number of chunks remaining until video end.
    Flattening this matrix yields a $48$-dimensional state vector that is
    fed into a two-layer fully connected neural network.
    The network outputs a softmax distribution over six discrete bitrate
    actions $a \in \{300,\, 750,\, 1200,\, 1850,\, 2850,\, 4300\}$~kbps.
    
    The model is trained to maximise a QoE-based reward
    \begin{equation}
        R = R_{\text{bitrate}}
            - 4.3 \cdot R_{\text{rebuffer}}
            - R_{\text{switch}},
        \label{eq:pensieve-reward}
    \end{equation}
    where $R_{\text{bitrate}}$ is the cumulative bitrate utility over recent
    chunks, $R_{\text{rebuffer}}$ is the total rebuffering time in seconds,
    and $R_{\text{switch}}$ is the sum of absolute differences between
    consecutive chunk bitrates.
    In empirical evaluations, Pensieve achieves QoE gains of $10$--$70\%$
    over traditional controllers~\cite{pensieve}, motivating widespread
    interest in ML-based ABR approaches.
    
    Crucially, Pensieve operates in a \emph{closed loop}: each selected
    action $a_t$ influences the next state $s_{t+1}$ through an environment
    transition function~$f$ (Equation~\ref{eq:closed-loop}).
    This closed-loop coupling means that any formal analysis of Pensieve
    must account not only for the neural controller itself but also for the
    dynamics of the environment in which it operates.
    
    
    % ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
    \subsection{Specification Mining with SpecTRA}
    \label{sec:bg-spectra}
    
    As noted in Section~\ref{subsec:problem}, ABR streaming lacks a
    universal correctness criterion.
    SpecTRA~\cite{spec-tra} fills this gap by mining
    \emph{pre-/post-condition specifications} directly from the controller's
    training traces.  Given logged input--output pairs from Pensieve,
    SpecTRA clusters the state space into operating regimes, extracts
    per-feature interval bounds $[\ell_j, u_j]$ as pre-conditions for each
    cluster, and summarises the dominant action $a^{\ast}$ as the
    post-condition.  A specification is thus a triple
    $(\text{cluster id},\; [\ell, u],\; a^{\ast})$: within the state
    region $[\ell, u]$, Pensieve is expected to select~$a^{\ast}$.
    
    These specifications serve two roles in this thesis: they constrain
    adversarial perturbations to physically plausible states
    (Chapter~\ref{chap:attack}), and they provide the initial input
    regions for the verification pipeline
    (Chapter~\ref{chap:verify}).
    
    
    % ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
    \subsection{Neural Network Verification with LiRPA}
    \label{sec:bg-lirpa}
    
    \emph{Linear Relaxation-based Perturbation Analysis} (LiRPA) is a
    family of methods for computing certified output bounds of neural
    networks over bounded input sets.
    Given an input region $[\ell, u] \subseteq \mathbb{R}^d$, LiRPA
    propagates linear relaxations of each layer's activation function
    through the network, producing sound over-approximations
    $[\underline{y},\, \overline{y}]$ of the true output range---that is,
    for every $x \in [\ell, u]$, the network output satisfies
    $\underline{y} \leq g(x) \leq \overline{y}$.
    
    The \texttt{auto\_LiRPA} library~\cite{auto-lirpa} implements several
    propagation strategies with different cost--tightness trade-offs:
    
    \paragraph{Interval Bound Propagation (IBP)}
    propagates axis-aligned interval bounds layer by layer.
    IBP is fast ($O(1)$ per neuron) but introduces substantial
    over-approximation at each non-linear activation, causing bounds to
    grow rapidly with network depth.
    
    \paragraph{CROWN~\cite{crown}}
    computes linear upper and lower bounding functions for each neuron by
    back-propagating affine relaxations from the output to the input layer.
    CROWN bounds are significantly tighter than IBP but require a backward
    pass for each output dimension.
    
    \paragraph{CROWN-Optimised (CROWN-Opt)}
    extends CROWN by treating the relaxation slopes of unstable ReLU neurons
    as learnable parameters optimised via gradient descent.
    This produces the tightest bounds among the three methods at the cost of
    multiple optimisation iterations.
    
    All three methods are \emph{sound}: the computed bounds provably contain
    the true output for every input in the region.
    In this thesis, LiRPA is the core bound-computation engine used in both
    the Pensieve verification phase and the environment verification phase
    of the pipeline described in Chapter~\ref{chap:verify}.
    
    
    % ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
    \subsection{Statistical Certification: Clopper--Pearson Intervals}
    \label{sec:bg-cp}
    
    The verification tools described in Section~\ref{sec:bg-lirpa} provide
    \emph{deterministic} soundness guarantees for neural network outputs.
    However, they require that every component in the pipeline be a
    differentiable neural network with known architecture and weights.
    The environment transition function $f$ does not satisfy this
    requirement: even though our neural approximation $f^{\ast}$ can be
    analysed via LiRPA, there is an irreducible gap between $f^{\ast}$ and
    the true dynamics~$f$.
    To quantify this gap rigorously, we turn to \emph{statistical
    certification}.
    
    The Clopper--Pearson method~\cite{clopper-pearson} provides exact
    binomial confidence intervals without parametric assumptions.
    Given $n$ i.i.d.\ test samples of which $k$ satisfy a binary success
    criterion, it models each sample as a Bernoulli trial with unknown
    success probability $p$ and computes
    \begin{equation}
        \mathrm{CI}_{1-\alpha}(p)
        = \left[
            \mathrm{Beta}^{-1}\!\!\left(\tfrac{\alpha}{2};\, k,\, n{-}k{+}1\right),\;\;
            \mathrm{Beta}^{-1}\!\!\left(1 - \tfrac{\alpha}{2};\, k{+}1,\, n{-}k\right)
        \right],
        \label{eq:cp}
    \end{equation}
    where $\alpha$ is the significance level and $\mathrm{Beta}^{-1}$ is
    the quantile function of the Beta distribution.
    The lower bound of this interval gives a conservative but rigorous
    guarantee on the true success probability~$p$.
    
    In Chapter~\ref{chap:env}, we define success as the event that
    $f^{\ast}$'s prediction incurs less than $10\%$ normalised error
    relative to the true next-state value.
    Applying the Clopper--Pearson method with $\alpha = 0.10$ yields a
    certified lower bound on the fraction of inputs for which $f^{\ast}$
    meets this accuracy threshold.
    This bound is then propagated into the multi-round verification pipeline
    (Chapter~\ref{chap:verify}), where a statistical compounding argument
    quantifies how prediction error accumulates across successive control
    rounds.

\section{Adversarial Vulnerability of Neural ABR Controllers}

    This chapter presents the first systematic study of adversarial attacks

    sufficient to induce significant QoE degradation in Pensieve,
    establishing a concrete threat model and motivating the formal
    verification framework developed in subsequent chapters.
    
    Section~\ref{sec:attack-prelim} reviews the adversarial robustness
    background needed for this chapter.
    Section~\ref{sec:attack-surface} characterises the attack surface of
    neural ABR controllers.
    Section~\ref{sec:threat-model} defines the threat model.
    Section~\ref{sec:attack-design} describes the specification-guided

    Section~\ref{sec:attack-eval} presents the experimental evaluation.

    % ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
    \subsection{Preliminaries: Adversarial Robustness and PGD}
    \label{sec:attack-prelim}
    
    Neural networks are known to be vulnerable to \emph{adversarial
    examples}: inputs that are imperceptibly perturbed yet cause the model
    to produce confident but incorrect outputs~\cite{attack}.
    In the image domain, a rich literature has developed around both attack
    algorithms---such as the Fast Gradient Sign Method (FGSM) and Projected
    Gradient Descent (PGD)~\cite{pgd}---and defences including adversarial
    training and certified robustness methods.
    
    PGD is an iterative first-order attack that computes adversarial
    perturbations by repeatedly taking gradient steps on the loss function
    and projecting the result back onto a prescribed perturbation set
    (typically an $\ell_p$-ball).
    Given input $x$, model $g$, and perturbation budget $\epsilon$, PGD
    iterates
    \begin{equation}
        x^{(k+1)} = \Pi_{\lVert \delta \rVert_p \leq \epsilon}
            \!\bigl(x^{(k)} + \alpha \cdot \mathrm{sign}\bigl(\nabla_x
            \mathcal{L}(g(x^{(k)}), y)\bigr)\bigr),
        \label{eq:pgd}
    \end{equation}
    where $\alpha$ is the step size, $\mathcal{L}$ is the loss, and
    $\Pi$ denotes projection onto the feasible set.
    
    While adversarial robustness has been studied extensively in
    classification settings, its implications for \emph{control} systems
    remain under-explored.
    In the ABR context, adversarial perturbations have a distinctive
    character: the attacker does not alter pixel intensities but rather
    manipulates observed network metrics (e.g.\ download times), and
    perturbations must remain physically realisable---for instance, they
    must respect the definitional coupling between throughput, chunk size,
    and download time.
    The remainder of this chapter develops a specification-guided PGD
    attack tailored to these constraints.
    
    
    % ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
    \subsection{Attack Surface of Neural ABR Controllers}
    \label{sec:attack-surface}
    
    Neural ABR policies expose a broad input attack surface, making them
    susceptible to manipulation at multiple layers of the network stack.
    We identify three representative adversary positions.
    
    \paragraph{Passive on-path adversary.}
    Because download time is measured at the client, any on-path
    device---such as a public Wi-Fi access point, a compromised home
    router, or an ISP-level traffic shaper---can inject jitter, delay
    packets, or disrupt acknowledgments to falsify perceived network
    performance.
    
    \paragraph{Active JavaScript adversary.}
    A malicious third-party advertisement running in the same browser tab
    can generate CPU contention or spawn background fetches that interfere
    with the ABR player's HTTP requests, degrading measured throughput.
    
    \paragraph{Content-side manipulator.}
    A compromised CDN edge server can alter server-side timestamps or
    response pacing to skew round-trip-time measurements, without modifying
    the encrypted video payload itself.
    
    None of these attack vectors requires access to the player binary or
    decryption of traffic.  By manipulating a small number of observable
    signals, an adversary can drive the neural controller toward poor
    bitrate decisions, leading to quality degradation that end users
    attribute to the streaming provider.  This asymmetric attribution makes
    ML-based ABR controllers especially attractive targets.
    
    
    % ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
    \subsection{Threat Model}
    \label{sec:threat-model}
    
    We consider a network-layer adversary capable of launching targeted
    Distributed Denial-of-Service (DDoS) attacks to degrade the
    performance of the ABR controller.  The attacker does not need to
    compromise the video player or the content delivery infrastructure;
    instead, it relies on disrupting network conditions to manipulate
    client-side observations.
    
    Potential targets for the DDoS attack include:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item \textbf{Video server:} introducing packet delays or throttling
            response times to inflate perceived download latency.
        \item \textbf{Home router or access link:} injecting congestion or
            cross-traffic near the user to induce higher variability in
            throughput and delay.
        \item \textbf{Other on-path infrastructure:} such as shared Wi-Fi
            access points, edge proxies, or ISP-level bottlenecks.
    \end{itemize}
    
    The attacker's primary goal is to increase the observed video download
    time for the most recent chunk.  By doing so, the attack steers the ABR
    policy toward lower bitrate selections, ultimately degrading QoE
    without needing to intercept or alter encrypted video content.
    
    
    % ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
    \subsection{Specification-Guided Attack Design}
    \label{sec:attack-design}
    
    This section describes the design of our adversarial attack pipeline.
    We first explain the role of specification guidance
    (Section~\ref{sec:attack-why-spec}), then detail two simplifications
    that enable tractable evaluation
    (Section~\ref{sec:attack-simplification}), describe how specifications
    are generated (Section~\ref{sec:attack-specgen}), and present the
    constrained PGD algorithm (Section~\ref{sec:attack-pgd}).
    
    
    % - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - -
    \subsubsection{Why Specification Guidance?}
    \label{sec:attack-why-spec}
    
    Unconstrained gradient-based attacks operate over the full input space
    and often generate physically invalid states---for example, negative
    throughput values or internal inconsistencies such as
    $\text{throughput} \times \text{download time} \neq \text{chunk size}$.
    Such states would either be rejected by sanity checks in the player or
    neutralised by built-in recovery logic.
    
    Incorporating SpecTRA-mined invariants into PGD transforms it into a
    \emph{specification-guided} attack, where each perturbation step is
    projected back onto the legal state space.  This design offers three
    advantages:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item \textbf{Plausibility.}  The resulting adversarial states
            closely resemble natural variations, making them harder to
            detect via simple anomaly checks.
        \item \textbf{Efficiency.}  The constrained search space reduces
            wasted gradient steps, accelerating convergence and avoiding
            updates that would be clipped away.
        \item \textbf{Generalisation.}  Staying within the manifold of
            realistic states increases the likelihood that adversarial
            examples remain effective when deployed in live systems with
            internal feature dependencies.
    \end{itemize}
    
    In short, specification-guided attacks combine the optimisation power of
    gradient methods with the realism needed to influence real-world
    controllers, bridging the gap between theoretical feasibility and
    practical exploitability.
    
    
    % - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - -
    \subsubsection{Simplifications}
    \label{sec:attack-simplification}
    
    We make two simplifications to enable tractable evaluation.
    
    \paragraph{Two-chunk video runs.}
    Instead of attacking an entire video session---where the number of
    chunks is user-dependent and varies in length---we treat a two-chunk
    segment as a complete video run.  This isolates the effect of a single
    adversarial perturbation on immediate bitrate selection and QoE.
    
    \paragraph{Exclusion of rebuffering.}
    Pensieve's dataset contains no rebuffering events across the sampled
    video runs, so our experiments omit this component from the QoE
    calculation.  Moreover, rebuffering behaviour is highly dependent on
    user interaction patterns (e.g.\ pause, seek, network switching), which
    are difficult to model accurately in a controlled attack scenario.
    
    Under these assumptions, the QoE formula for a video run becomes
    \begin{equation}
        \text{QoE}
            = q(R_1) + q(R_2) - \lvert q(R_1) - q(R_2) \rvert
            = 2 \cdot \min\{q(R_1),\, q(R_2)\},
        \label{eq:attack-qoe}
    \end{equation}
    where $R_1$ and $R_2$ are the bitrates selected for the two chunks and
    $q(\cdot)$ is a logarithmic utility function mapping bitrate to
    perceived quality:
    \begin{equation}
        q(R) = \log\!\left(\frac{R}{R_{\min}}\right),
        \label{eq:utility}
    \end{equation}
    with $R_{\min} = 300$~kbps denoting the minimum available bitrate in
    Pensieve.  This formulation captures the diminishing returns of higher
    bitrates: increasing bitrate improves quality, but the perceptual gain
    becomes less pronounced at higher values.
    
    
    % - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - -
    \subsubsection{Specification Generation}
    \label{sec:attack-specgen}
    
    We use SpecTRA~\cite{spec-tra} to automatically generate input and
    output specifications for Pensieve.  SpecTRA analyses logged training
    traces and extracts concrete pre-/post-condition bounds that serve two
    purposes in the attack pipeline: they define a valid state manifold,
    ensuring that perturbed inputs remain physically plausible and
    consistent with real-world network behaviour, and they restrict the
    optimisation space of PGD, enabling faster convergence and more stealthy
    perturbations.
    
    \begin{figure}[t]
        \centering
        % TODO: include the SpecTRA specification figure from the paper
        % \includegraphics[width=0.85\linewidth,alt={Example SpecTRA
        %   specification showing pre-condition bounds on selected input
        %   features and post-condition constraints on output bitrate and
        %   QoE.}]{figures/spectra_spec.pdf}
        \caption{Example specification generated by SpecTRA, showing
            pre-condition bounds on selected input features and
            post-condition constraints on output bitrate and QoE.}
        \label{fig:spectra-spec}
    \end{figure}
    
    For example, as shown in Figure~\ref{fig:spectra-spec}, SpecTRA
    provides pre-condition bounds on selected input features and
    post-condition constraints on output behaviour.  The pre-condition
    specifies that \texttt{Chunks\_left} must lie in the range $[0.0,\,
    9.6]$, \texttt{Last1\_buffer\_size} in $[11.6,\, 15.1]$,
    \texttt{Last1\_chunk\_bitrate} is fixed at $2850$~kbps, and
    \texttt{Last1\_downloadtime} lies in $[1.5,\, 7.9]$.  The
    post-condition defines allowable values for the controller's output
    bitrate and expected QoE, restricting the policy to a subset of
    possible actions and outcomes.  These constraints are encoded into the
    attack pipeline as projection bounds to ensure that all adversarial
    inputs remain within the feasible region.
    
    
    % - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - -
    \subsubsection{SpecTRA-Constrained PGD Attack}
    \label{sec:attack-pgd}
    
    We implement a targeted adversarial attack using PGD.  The goal is to
    apply minimal perturbations to the ABR state input in order to force
    the neural policy to select the lowest bitrate ($300$~kbps), thereby
    minimising QoE.
    
    At each step, PGD performs gradient ascent on the targeted loss
    function (cross-entropy with respect to the $300$~kbps class), followed
    by projection back onto a feasible region defined by both an
    $\ell_\infty$-norm budget and the physical bounds mined by SpecTRA.
    To maintain stealth and physical plausibility, we apply PGD to only one
    input feature: \texttt{Last1\_downloadtime}.
    
    The attack proceeds iteratively as shown in
    Algorithm~\ref{alg:pgd-attack}.  In each iteration, the value of
    \texttt{Last1\_throughput} is updated to remain consistent with the
    perturbed \texttt{Last1\_downloadtime}, preserving the relationship
    between throughput and download time and ensuring input realism.
    
    \begin{algorithm}[t]
        \caption{SpecTRA-Constrained PGD Attack}
        \label{alg:pgd-attack}
        \begin{algorithmic}[1]
            \Require Initial state $\mathbf{x}$, model $\pi_\theta$, step
                    size $\alpha$, perturbation bound $\epsilon$,
                    specification bounds $\mathbf{B}_{\text{low}}$,
                    $\mathbf{B}_{\text{high}}$, iterations $T$
            \Ensure  Adversarial state $\tilde{\mathbf{x}}^{(T)}$
            \State Initialise: $\tilde{\mathbf{x}}^{(0)} \gets \mathbf{x}$
            \For{$t = 0$ \textbf{to} $T - 1$}
                \State Compute gradient:
                    $\mathbf{g} \gets \nabla_{\tilde{\mathbf{x}}}
                        \mathcal{L}(\tilde{\mathbf{x}}^{(t)},\, y = 0)$
                \State Gradient step:
                    $\tilde{\mathbf{x}}^{(t+\frac{1}{2})} \gets
                        \tilde{\mathbf{x}}^{(t)} + \alpha \cdot
                        \mathrm{sign}(\mathbf{g})$
                \State Project to $\ell_\infty$ ball:
                    $\tilde{\mathbf{x}}^{(t+\frac{1}{2})} \gets
                        \mathrm{clip}\bigl(
                        \tilde{\mathbf{x}}^{(t+\frac{1}{2})},\;
                        \mathbf{x} - \epsilon,\;
                        \mathbf{x} + \epsilon\bigr)$
                \State Project to SpecTRA bounds:
                    $\tilde{\mathbf{x}}^{(t+1)} \gets
                        \mathrm{clip}\bigl(
                        \tilde{\mathbf{x}}^{(t+\frac{1}{2})},\;
                        \mathbf{B}_{\text{low}},\;
                        \mathbf{B}_{\text{high}}\bigr)$
            \EndFor
            \State \Return $\tilde{\mathbf{x}}^{(T)}$
        \end{algorithmic}
    \end{algorithm}
    
    This attack is simple, fast to compute, and leverages domain
    constraints to ensure that adversarial inputs are realistic and
    plausible under real network conditions.
    
    
    % ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
    \subsection{Evaluation}
    \label{sec:attack-eval}
    
    We evaluate the attack against the Pensieve ABR agent to assess its
    vulnerability to specification-guided adversarial perturbations.
    
    
    % - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - -
    \subsubsection{Experimental Setup}
    \label{sec:attack-setup}
    
    We evaluate the attack against a single SpecTRA-derived specification
    using $30{,}000$ test samples randomly generated from the
    specification.  A test sample is considered a \emph{successful attack}
    if the selected bitrate both degrades relative to the unperturbed
    decision and falls outside the nominal output range defined by the
    post-condition.
    
    
    % - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - -
    \subsubsection{Metrics}
    \label{sec:attack-metrics}
    
    We evaluate our attack using three primary metrics:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item \textbf{Attack success rate:} the percentage of states where
            the selected bitrate is both degraded and falls outside the
            nominal output range defined by the specification (i.e.\
            out-of-bound).
        \item \textbf{Average bitrate reduction:} the average decrease in
            selected bitrate across successfully attacked states, compared
            to the original, unperturbed decisions.
        \item \textbf{Average QoE loss:} the mean reduction in QoE
            resulting from the attack.
    \end{itemize}
    These metrics collectively capture both the effectiveness and
    perceptual impact of the attack on the ABR agent's decision-making.
    
    
    % - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - -

    \label{sec:attack-results}

    bounds defined by the specification and led to degraded quality.

    \subsubsection{Attack Stealth}
    \label{sec:attack-stealth}
    
    All perturbations are limited to a $10\%$ increase in download time,
    which falls within normal network variance and is unlikely to trigger
    anomaly detectors that monitor coarse throughput trends.  Because the
    perturbed states remain inside the SpecTRA-derived valid state manifold,
    they are indistinguishable from natural network fluctuations when
    assessed by standard sanity checks.
    
    
    % ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
    \subsection{Limitations}
    \label{sec:attack-limitations}
    
    While the attack demonstrates the feasibility of adversarial
    degradation in ML-based ABR systems, it is subject to several
    simplifying assumptions and scoped constraints.
    
    \paragraph{Narrow attack surface.}
    The attack modifies only one input feature:
    \texttt{Last1\_downloadtime}.  In real-world scenarios, adversaries may
    be able to manipulate multiple correlated features (e.g.\ throughput,
    buffer size), potentially yielding higher success rates and more
    nuanced attack vectors.
    
    \paragraph{Simplified QoE modelling.}
    We assume a fixed number of video chunks and exclude rebuffering from
    the QoE calculation.  This decision is based on the absence of
    rebuffering events in the Pensieve dataset, as well as the difficulty
    of modelling user-driven behaviours (e.g.\ pause, seek).  However, this
    abstraction limits realism and may underestimate certain types of
    degradation.
    
    \paragraph{Single specification constraint.}
    The evaluation uses a single specification derived from Pensieve's
    training trace.  This restricts the applicability of our findings to one
    particular operational regime.  Broader generalisation across multiple
    specifications, content types, and runtime environments remains
    unexplored.
    
    These limitations highlight key avenues for future work, including
    multi-feature attacks, more expressive QoE models, and wider validation
    across modern ABR systems and network conditions.  Importantly, the
    vulnerabilities exposed here---even under conservative
    assumptions---motivate the formal verification framework developed in
    the remainder of this thesis.
    
    
    % ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
    \subsection{Summary}
    \label{sec:attack-summary}
    
    This chapter presented the first systematic study showing that ML-based
    ABR controllers can be compromised by small, in-bound perturbations.
    Using SpecTRA to extract realistic specifications, we embedded those
    constraints in a PGD attack that modifies only one
    feature---\texttt{Last1\_downloadtime}---by at most $10\%$.  This
    lightweight manipulation is sufficient to drive Pensieve to select the
    lowest bitrate in $1.9\%$ of $30{,}000$ test states, reducing average
    bitrate by $35$--$65\%$ and QoE by $30$--$40\%$ across successful
    attacks.  Because the perturbations stay inside an empirically valid
    state manifold, they evade simple sanity checks, exposing a practical
    security risk for neural systems deployments.
    
    These findings establish two concrete motivations for the subsequent
    chapters.  First, the fact that Pensieve is vulnerable to small
    perturbations underscores the need for a trustworthy environment model
    that can support formal reasoning about worst-case behaviour
    (Chapter~\ref{chap:env}).  Second, the specification-guided attack
    framework provides the perturbation sets and correctness criteria that
    seed the end-to-end verification pipeline
    (Chapter~\ref{chap:verify}).
