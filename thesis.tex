\abstract{
    % This is the abstract of the thesis. It should be a concise summary of the
    % research, including the main findings and conclusions. The abstract should
    % be no more than 350 words in length.

    Machine-learning-based adaptive bitrate (ABR) controllers such as
    Pensieve~\cite{pensieve} have demonstrated substantial Quality of
    Experience (QoE) improvements over hand-crafted heuristics, yet
    their black-box nature introduces adversarial vulnerability and
    precludes formal correctness guarantees.  This thesis addresses
    both through three contributions.

    We first show that Pensieve is susceptible to adversarial attack.
    Using specifications mined by SpecTRA~\cite{spec-tra}, we
    construct a PGD attack that perturbs only the most recent download
    time by at most $10\%$, reducing the selected bitrate by
    $35$--$65\%$ and degrading QoE by $30$--$40\%$ across $581$
    successful attacks out of $30{,}000$ test states.

    To enable formal verification of the closed-loop system, we train
    a neural environment predictor $f^{\ast}$ on real-world datasets, 
    collected by Puffer~\cite{fugu}, and certify it via Clopper--Pearson
    intervals~\cite{clopper-pearson}: at least $98.46\%$ of predictions
    incur less than $10\%$ normalised error ($90\%$ confidence).

    Building on $f^{\ast}$, we design the first end-to-end verification
    pipeline that alternates between a \emph{Pensieve phase}
    (auto\_LiRPA~\cite{auto-lirpa} bound propagation with
    branch-and-bound search) and an \emph{environment phase}
    ($f^{\ast}$ interval propagation).  Iterating over multiple rounds
    builds a hierarchical reachability tree~\cite{tree} whose paths enumerate all
    verified action sequences with associated $\Delta$QoE (per-step change in QoE) at each
    transition, with formal soundness guarantees and a statistical
    compounding bound ($p^T \geq 0.9246$ for $T = 5$).
    The pipeline provides a general methodology for formally reasoning
    about worst-case multi-round behaviour of neural controllers in
    closed-loop networked systems.  We demonstrate it on five
    SpecTRA-derived specifications, where the reachability trees and
    their $\Delta$QoE annotations expose how initial action selection
    and action-sequence structure determine cumulative QoE outcomes.


}

\section{Introduction}

    \label{chap:intro}
 
    Online video accounts for the dominant share of global Internet
    traffic~\cite{cloudinary-quality}.  To deliver smooth playback over
    volatile network links, modern streaming players employ an
    \emph{adaptive bitrate} (ABR) controller that selects
    the video quality level for each chunk based on current network
    conditions, balancing video quality, rebuffering risk, and
    quality-switch penalties into a single Quality of Experience (QoE)
    objective~\cite{pensieve}.

    For decades, ABR controllers were built from hand-crafted heuristics.
    Rule-based algorithms such as BOLA~\cite{bola} and
    MPC~\cite{mpc} are interpretable but cannot capture
    the complex, nonlinear interactions among bandwidth variability,
    buffer dynamics, and chunk sizes.  Pensieve~\cite{pensieve} addresses
    this gap with a neural network trained via reinforcement
    learning (RL) to maximise QoE directly, achieving
    gains of $10$--$70\%$ over rule-based baselines.  Other ML-based
    approaches, such as Fugu~\cite{fugu}, combine a supervised throughput
    predictor with model-predictive control to similar effect.

    This performance advantage comes at a fundamental cost.  Neural
    networks are opaque: their decision boundaries are encoded in
    millions of floating-point parameters, making their behaviour under
    worst-case inputs difficult to reason about or formally verify.
    Research in computer vision has shown that small, carefully
    crafted perturbations can cause neural networks to produce confident
    but incorrect outputs~\cite{attack}.  This thesis demonstrates that
    the same fragility afflicts neural ABR controllers, and develops
    the \emph{first} end-to-end verification pipeline that certifies closed-loop
    QoE behaviour by composing neural-network verification with a
    statistically certified environment model, producing reachability
    trees~\cite{tree} whose paths enumerate all verified action sequences with
    associated $\Delta$QoE bounds (defined in \ref{chap:verify}).

    % ---- 1.1  Problem Statement and Challenges ----------------------------
    \subsection{Problem Statement and Challenges}
    \label{subsec:problem}
 
    The overarching goal of this thesis is to develop a principled
    framework for formally reasoning about the closed-loop QoE behaviour
    of neural ABR controllers.  Achieving this requires overcoming
    three challenges that go beyond classical neural network
    verification.
 
    \paragraph{Challenge~1: Multi-round bound propagation.}
    Traditional neural network verification targets a single forward
    pass through one network.  QoE verification, by contrast, must
    reason over \emph{multiple control rounds}.  The system evolves as
    \begin{equation}
        s_{t+1} = f(s_t,\, a_t), \qquad a_t = \mathrm{NN}(s_t),
        \label{eq:closed-loop}
    \end{equation}
    where each round's output bounds become the next round's input
    bounds.  Over-approximation errors therefore compound across the
    trajectory.  Managing this accumulation while keeping the analysis
    tractable is a core design challenge of the verification pipeline
    developed in \ref{chap:verify}.

    \paragraph{Challenge~2: Unknown environment dynamics.}
    Pensieve operates in a \emph{closed loop}: each bitrate decision
    shapes the future network state the controller will observe.
    The neural controller $\mathrm{NN}$ can be analysed with existing
    verification tools, but the environment transition function~$f$
    encodes stochastic network dynamics whose exact form is unknown.
    Any formal guarantee about the \emph{system}, rather than the
    controller in isolation, requires a trustworthy, bounded model
    of~$f$ that can be composed with the controller verification at
    each round. Prior work~\cite{ouroboros, c3} on
    neural-network-based systems (NN4Sys)~\cite{congestion-control, pensieve}
    verification has left this gap unaddressed.

    \paragraph{Challenge~3: Physical coupling of input features.}
    Pensieve's input features are not independent.  Throughput, for
    example, is definitionally chunk size divided by download time.
    Perturbations applied to one feature must respect these physical
    dependencies to remain realisable; otherwise, adversarial analyses
    and verification results describe states that can never arise in
    practice.  Both our attack construction
    (\ref{chap:attack}) and our verification pipeline
    (\ref{chap:verify}) enforce such consistency constraints.
 

    % ---- 1.2  Contributions -----------------------------------------------
    \subsection{Contributions}
    \label{subsec:contributions}
 
    This thesis makes three contributions that together form an
    end-to-end framework for formally reasoning about Pensieve's
    closed-loop QoE behaviour.  To the best of our knowledge, this is
    the first work to perform multi-round QoE verification and analysis
    for neural ABR controllers.
 
    \paragraph{Adversarial vulnerability analysis
               (\ref{chap:attack}).}
    We present the first systematic study of adversarial attacks on
    ML-based ABR controllers.  Using SpecTRA~\cite{spec-tra}-derived specifications to
    constrain perturbations to physically valid states, we construct a
    PGD attack that perturbs only the most recent download time by at
    most $10\%$.  Across $30{,}000$ test states, the attack finds $581$
    adversarial examples that reduce the selected bitrate by
    $35$--$65\%$ and degrade single-round QoE by $30$--$40\%$, while
    remaining within empirically valid network bounds.  These results
    establish a concrete threat model and motivate the need for formal
    verification for QoE analysis.

    \paragraph{Statistically certified environment model
               (\ref{chap:env}).}
    We construct a neural predictor $f^{\ast}$ for the
    environment transition function~$f$, trained on real-world traces
    from the Puffer dataset~\cite{fugu} and equipped with
    Clopper--Pearson confidence intervals~\cite{clopper-pearson}.
    Our best model achieves a certified lower bound of
    $0.9846$ at $90\%$ confidence: at least $98.46\%$ of predictions
    incur less than $10\%$ normalised error.  This certified predictor
    provides a reliable approximation of the unkown network transition dynamics
    for multi-round neurosymbolic ABR controlled video streaming systems.

    \paragraph{End-to-end multi-round QoE verification pipeline
               (\ref{chap:verify}).}
    We design a novel multi-round verification pipeline that alternates
    between two phases.  In the \emph{Pensieve phase},
    auto\_LiRPA~\cite{auto-lirpa} bound propagation and a parallel
    branch-and-bound search partition the input region into verified
    subregions, each paired with a single verified action.  In the
    \emph{environment phase}, $f^{\ast}$ propagates interval bounds
    forward to obtain sound next-state input bounds.  Iterating across
    rounds produces a hierarchical reachability tree that enumerates
    all verified action sequences, each annotated with a $\Delta$QoE
    score (defined in \ref{chap:verify}) at every action transition.
    The tree provides exhaustive, formally sound coverage of the
    controller's multi-round QoE behaviour.  We further establish a
    statistical compounding bound quantifying how prediction error
    accumulates across rounds.

    \subsection{Video Streaming and the Pensieve Controller}
    \label{subsec:streaming}
    
    Online video has become the dominant load on the public Internet,
    accounting for the majority of consumer IP
    traffic~\cite{cloudinary-quality}.
    To keep playback smooth over volatile links, nearly all modern streaming
    players employ an ABR controller.
    Video content is delivered in discrete \emph{chunks}, each typically
    $2$--$4$~seconds of playback.
    After downloading each chunk, the client uses recent network
    metrics---throughput and buffer occupancy, among others---to select the
    quality level of the next chunk.
    
    Conventional ABR schemes such as \emph{BOLA}~\cite{bola} and
    \emph{MPC}~\cite{mpc} encode expert-designed rules; for example,
    ``maintain at least $B$~seconds of buffered video, then select the
    highest bitrate whose predicted download time is less than $B - C$,''
    where $B$ and $C$ are hand-tuned constants.
    While these heuristics perform reasonably under typical conditions, they
    struggle to capture the complex, nonlinear relationships among bandwidth
    variability, buffer dynamics, and chunk size.
    
    The primary objective of an ABR controller is to maximise QoE, which
    balances three factors: video quality (higher bitrates improve visual
    fidelity but increase download time and rebuffering risk), rebuffering
    (playback stalls severely degrade user experience), and quality switches
    (frequent bitrate changes are perceptually jarring).
    A standard QoE metric combines these factors as
    \begin{equation}
        \mathrm{QoE}
            = \sum_t q(R_t)
            - \mu \sum_t B_t
            - \nu \sum_t \lvert q(R_t) - q(R_{t-1}) \rvert,
        \label{eq:qoe}
    \end{equation}
    where $q(R_t)$ is the bitrate utility of the selected quality level,
    $B_t$ is the rebuffering duration, and $\mu$, $\nu$ are penalty
    weights that encode deployment-specific preferences.
    A common choice for the utility function is the logarithmic form
    \begin{equation}
        q(R) = \log\!\left(\frac{R}{R_{\min}}\right),
        \label{eq:bg-utility}
    \end{equation}
    where $R_{\min}$ is the minimum available bitrate, capturing the
    diminishing perceptual returns of higher bitrates.

    \paragraph{Pensieve.}
    Pensieve~\cite{pensieve} replaces hand-crafted heuristics with a neural
    policy trained via RL.
    At each decision point the player constructs a $6 \times 8$ state matrix
    $\mathbf{S}$ whose rows capture:
    (i)~past throughput samples,
    (ii)~past download times,
    (iii)~past buffer occupancy levels,
    (iv)~past selected bitrates,
    (v)~available chunk sizes at all quality levels, and
    (vi)~the number of chunks remaining until video end.
    Flattening this matrix yields a $48$-dimensional state vector that is
    fed into a two-layer fully connected neural network.
    The network outputs a softmax distribution over six discrete bitrate
    actions $a \in \{300,\, 750,\, 1200,\, 1850,\, 2850,\, 4300\}$~kbps.
    
    The model is trained to maximise a QoE-based reward
    \begin{equation}
        R = R_{\text{bitrate}}
            - 4.3 \cdot R_{\text{rebuffer}}
            - R_{\text{switch}},
        \label{eq:pensieve-reward}
    \end{equation}
    where $R_{\text{bitrate}}$ is the cumulative bitrate utility over recent
    chunks, $R_{\text{rebuffer}}$ is the total rebuffering time in seconds,
    and $R_{\text{switch}}$ is the sum of absolute differences between
    consecutive chunk bitrates. Pensieve achieves QoE gains of $10$--$70\%$
    over traditional controllers~\cite{pensieve}, motivating widespread
    interest in ML-based ABR approaches.
    
    Crucially, Pensieve operates in a \emph{closed loop}: each selected
    action $a_t$ influences the next state $s_{t+1}$ through an environment
    transition function~$f$ (Equation~\ref{eq:closed-loop}).
    This closed-loop coupling means that any formal analysis of Pensieve
    and the neurosymbolic system under which it operates must account 
    not only for the neural controller itself but also for the
    dynamics of the environment in which it operates.
    
    
    % ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
    \subsection{Specification Mining with SpecTRA}
    \label{sec:bg-spectra}
    
    As noted in Section~\ref{subsec:problem}, ABR streaming lacks a
    universal correctness criterion.
    SpecTRA~\cite{spec-tra} fills this gap by mining
    \emph{pre-/post-condition specifications} directly from the controller's
    training traces.  Given logged input--output pairs from Pensieve,
    SpecTRA clusters the state space into operating regimes, extracts
    per-feature interval bounds $[\ell_j, u_j]$ as pre-conditions for each
    cluster, and summarises the dominant action $a^{\ast}$ as the
    post-condition.  A specification is thus a triple
    $(\text{cluster id},\; [\ell, u],\; a^{\ast})$: within the state
    region $[\ell, u]$, Pensieve is expected to select~$a^{\ast}$.
    
    These specifications serve two roles in this thesis: they constrain
    adversarial perturbations to physically plausible states
    (\ref{chap:attack}), and they provide the initial input
    regions for the experiments described in \ref{chap:verify}.
    
    
    % ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
    \subsection{Neural Network Verification with LiRPA}
    \label{sec:bg-lirpa}
    
    \emph{Linear Relaxation-based Perturbation Analysis} (LiRPA) is a
    family of \emph{sound} methods for computing output bounds of
    neural networks over bounded input sets.  Soundness means the
    computed bounds are guaranteed to contain the true output for
    every input in the region.  Given a neural network
    $g : \mathbb{R}^d \to \mathbb{R}^m$ and an input region
    $[\ell, u] \subseteq \mathbb{R}^d$, LiRPA propagates linear
    relaxations of each layer's activation function through the
    network, producing output bounds
    $\underline{y},\, \overline{y} \in \mathbb{R}^m$ such that
    $\underline{y} \leq g(x) \leq \overline{y}$ for all $x \in [\ell, u]$.

    The auto\_LiRPA library~\cite{auto-lirpa} implements several
    propagation strategies with different cost--tightness trade-offs:

    \paragraph{Interval Bound Propagation (IBP)~\cite{ibp}}
    propagates axis-aligned interval bounds layer by layer.
    IBP is fast ($O(n)$ per neuron) but over-approximation grows with depth.

    \paragraph{CROWN~\cite{crown}}
    back-propagates affine relaxations from output to input layer,
    producing tighter bounds than IBP at the cost of a backward pass per output dimension.

    \paragraph{CROWN-Optimised (CROWN-Opt)~\cite{crown-opt}}
    extends CROWN by optimising the relaxation slopes of unstable
    ReLU neurons via gradient descent, producing the tightest bounds
    among the three methods.  A ReLU neuron $\max(0, z)$ is
    \emph{unstable} when its input bounds span zero
    ($\underline{z} < 0 < \overline{z}$), so the neuron may be
    either active or inactive depending on the input.

    In this thesis, LiRPA is the core bound-computation engine for the
    verification pipeline described in \ref{chap:verify}.
    
    
    % ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
    \subsection{Statistical Certification: Clopper--Pearson Intervals}
    \label{sec:bg-cp}
    
    The verification tools described in Section~\ref{sec:bg-lirpa} provide
    \emph{deterministic} soundness guarantees for neural network outputs.
    However, they require that every component in the pipeline be a
    differentiable neural network with known architecture and weights.
    The environment transition function $f$ does not satisfy this
    requirement: even though our neural approximation $f^{\ast}$ can be
    analysed via LiRPA, there is an irreducible gap between $f^{\ast}$ and
    the true dynamics~$f$.
    To quantify this gap rigorously, we turn to \emph{statistical
    certification}.
    
    The Clopper--Pearson method~\cite{clopper-pearson} provides exact
    binomial confidence intervals without parametric assumptions.
    Given $n$ i.i.d.\ test samples of which $k$ satisfy a binary success
    criterion, it models each sample as a Bernoulli trial with unknown
    success probability $p$ and computes
    \begin{equation}
        \mathrm{CI}_{1-\alpha}(p)
        = \left[
            \mathrm{Beta}^{-1}\!\!\left(\tfrac{\alpha}{2};\, k,\, n{-}k{+}1\right),\;\;
            \mathrm{Beta}^{-1}\!\!\left(1 - \tfrac{\alpha}{2};\, k{+}1,\, n{-}k\right)
        \right],
        \label{eq:cp}
    \end{equation}
    where $\alpha$ is the significance level and $\mathrm{Beta}^{-1}$ is
    the quantile function of the Beta distribution.
    The lower bound of this interval gives a conservative but rigorous
    guarantee on the true success probability~$p$.
    
    We apply this method to certify the environment model in
    \ref{chap:env} and to derive a statistical compounding bound
    for multi-round verification in \ref{chap:verify}.

\section{Adversarial Vulnerability of Neural ABR Controllers}
\label{chap:attack}
 
    This chapter presents the first systematic study of adversarial attacks
    on ML-based ABR controllers.  We show that a lightweight,
    specification-guided perturbation of a single input feature is sufficient
    to induce significant QoE degradation in Pensieve. This establishes a
    concrete threat model and motivates the formal end-to-end verification framework
    developed in subsequent chapters.

    background needed for this chapter.
    Section~\ref{sec:attack-surface} characterises the attack surface of

    Section~\ref{sec:threat-model} defines the threat model.
    Section~\ref{sec:attack-design} describes the specification-guided

    Section~\ref{sec:attack-eval} presents the experimental evaluation.

    % ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
    \subsection{Preliminaries: Adversarial Robustness and PGD}
    \label{sec:attack-prelim}
    
    Neural networks are known to be vulnerable to \emph{adversarial
    examples}: inputs that are imperceptibly perturbed yet cause the model
    to produce confident but incorrect outputs~\cite{attack}.
    In the image domain, a rich literature has developed around both attack
    algorithms such as the Fast Gradient Sign Method
    (FGSM)~\cite{fgsm} and Projected Gradient Descent
    (PGD)~\cite{pgd}, and defences including adversarial
    training~\cite{adversarial-training} and certified robustness
    methods~\cite{certified-robustness}.
    
    PGD is an iterative first-order attack that computes adversarial
    perturbations by repeatedly taking gradient steps on the loss function
    and projecting the result back onto a prescribed perturbation set
    (typically an $\ell_p$-ball).
    Given input $x$, true label $y$, model $g$, and perturbation
    budget $\epsilon$, PGD iterates
    \begin{equation}
        x^{(k+1)} = \Pi_{\lVert \delta \rVert_p \leq \epsilon}
            \!\bigl(x^{(k)} + \alpha \cdot \mathrm{sign}\bigl(\nabla_x
            \mathcal{L}(g(x^{(k)}), y)\bigr)\bigr),
        \label{eq:pgd}
    \end{equation}
    where $\alpha$ is the step size, $\mathcal{L}$ is the loss, and
    $\Pi$ denotes projection onto the feasible set.
    
    While adversarial robustness has been studied extensively in
    classification settings, its implications for \emph{control} systems
    remain under-explored.
    In the ABR context, adversarial perturbations have a distinctive
    character: the attacker does not alter pixel intensities but rather
    manipulates observed network metrics (e.g.\ download times), and
    perturbations must remain physically realisable---for instance, they
    must respect the definitional coupling between throughput, chunk size,
    and download time.
    The remainder of this chapter develops a specification-guided PGD
    attack tailored to these constraints.
    
    
    % ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
    \subsection{Attack Surface of Neural ABR Controllers}
    \label{sec:attack-surface}
    
    Neural ABR policies expose a broad attack surface, making them
    susceptible to manipulation at multiple layers of the network stack.
    We identify three representative adversary network positions.
    
    \paragraph{Passive on-path adversary.}~\cite{on-path}
    Because download time is measured at the client, any on-path
    device---such as a public Wi-Fi access point, a compromised home
    router, or an ISP-level traffic shaper---can inject jitter, delay
    packets, or disrupt acknowledgments to falsify perceived network
    performance.
    
    \paragraph{Active JavaScript adversary.}~\cite{js-attack}
    A malicious third-party advertisement running in the same browser tab
    can generate CPU contention or spawn background fetches that interfere
    with the ABR player's HTTP requests, degrading measured throughput.
    
    \paragraph{Content-side manipulator.}~\cite{cdn-attack}
    A compromised CDN edge server can alter server-side timestamps or
    response pacing to skew round-trip-time measurements, without modifying
    the encrypted video payload itself.
    
    None of these attack vectors requires access to the player binary or
    decryption of traffic.  By manipulating a small number of observable
    signals, an adversary can drive the neural controller toward poor
    bitrate decisions, leading to quality degradation that end users
    attribute to the streaming provider.  This asymmetric attribution makes
    ML-based ABR controllers especially attractive targets.
    
    
    % ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
    \subsection{Threat Model}
    \label{sec:threat-model}
    
    We consider a network-layer adversary capable of launching targeted
    Distributed Denial-of-Service~\cite{ddos} (DDoS) attacks to degrade the
    performance of the ABR controller.  The attacker does not need to
    compromise the video player or the content delivery infrastructure;
    instead, it relies on disrupting network conditions to manipulate
    client-side observations.
    
    Potential targets for the DDoS attack include:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item \textbf{Video server:} introducing packet delays or throttling
            response times to inflate perceived download latency.
        \item \textbf{Home router or access link:} injecting congestion or
            cross-traffic near the user to induce higher variability in
            throughput and delay.
        \item \textbf{Other on-path infrastructure:} such as shared Wi-Fi
            access points, edge proxies, or ISP-level bottlenecks.
    \end{itemize}
    
    The attacker's primary goal is to increase the observed download
    time for the most recent chunk.  By doing so, the attack steers the
    ABR policy toward lower bitrate selections, degrading QoE without
    needing to intercept or alter encrypted video content.

    \subsection{Specification-Guided Attack Design}
    \label{sec:attack-design}
    
    This section describes the design of our adversarial attack pipeline.
    We first explain the role of specification guidance
    (Section~\ref{sec:attack-why-spec}), then detail two simplifications
    that enable tractable evaluation
    (Section~\ref{sec:attack-simplification}), describe how specifications
    are generated (Section~\ref{sec:attack-specgen}), and present the
    constrained PGD algorithm (Section~\ref{sec:attack-pgd}).
    
    
    % - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - -
    \subsubsection{Why Specification Guidance?}
    \label{sec:attack-why-spec}
    
    Unconstrained gradient-based attacks operate over the full input space
    and often generate physically invalid states---for example, negative
    throughput values or internal inconsistencies such as
    $\text{throughput} \times \text{download time} \neq \text{chunk size}$.
    Such states would either be rejected by sanity checks in the player or
    neutralised by built-in recovery logic.
    
    Incorporating SpecTRA-mined invariants into PGD transforms it into a
    \emph{specification-guided} attack, where each perturbation step is
    projected back onto the legal state space.  This design offers three
    advantages:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item \textbf{Plausibility.}  The resulting adversarial states
            closely resemble natural variations, making them harder to
            detect via simple anomaly checks.
        \item \textbf{Efficiency.}  The constrained search space reduces
            wasted gradient steps, accelerating convergence and avoiding
            updates that would be clipped away.
        \item \textbf{Generalisation.}  Staying within the manifold of
            realistic states increases the likelihood that adversarial
            examples remain effective when deployed in live systems with
            internal feature dependencies.
    \end{itemize}
    
    In short, specification-guided attacks combine the optimisation power of
    gradient methods with the realism needed to influence real-world
    controllers, bridging the gap between theoretical feasibility and
    practical exploitability.
    
    
    % - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - -
    \subsubsection{Simplifications}
    \label{sec:attack-simplification}
    
    We make two simplifications to enable tractable evaluation.
    
    \paragraph{Two-chunk video runs.}
    Instead of attacking an entire video session, where the number of
    chunks is user-dependent and varies in length, we treat a two-chunk
    segment as a complete video run.  This isolates the effect of a single
    adversarial perturbation on immediate bitrate selection and QoE.
    
    \paragraph{Exclusion of rebuffering.}
    Pensieve's dataset contains no rebuffering events across the sampled
    video runs, so our experiments omit this component from the QoE
    calculation.  Moreover, rebuffering behaviour is highly dependent on
    user interaction patterns (e.g.\ pause, seek, network switching), which
    are difficult to model accurately in a controlled attack scenario like ours.
    
    Under these assumptions, the QoE formula for a video run becomes
    \begin{equation}
        \text{QoE}
            = q(R_1) + q(R_2) - \lvert q(R_1) - q(R_2) \rvert
            = 2 \cdot \min\{q(R_1),\, q(R_2)\},
        \label{eq:attack-qoe}
    \end{equation}
    where $R_1$ and $R_2$ are the bitrates selected for the two chunks and
    $q(\cdot)$ is a logarithmic utility function mapping bitrate to
    perceived quality:
    \begin{equation}
        q(R) = \log\!\left(\frac{R}{R_{\min}}\right),
        \label{eq:utility}
    \end{equation}
    with $R_{\min} = 300$~kbps denoting the minimum available bitrate in
    Pensieve.  This formulation captures the diminishing returns of higher
    bitrates: increasing bitrate improves quality, but the perceptual gain
    becomes less pronounced at higher values.
    
    
    % - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - -
    \subsubsection{Specification Generation}
    \label{sec:attack-specgen}
    
    We use SpecTRA~\cite{spec-tra} to automatically generate input and
    output specifications for Pensieve.  SpecTRA analyses logged training
    traces and extracts concrete pre-/post-condition bounds that serve two
    purposes in the attack pipeline: they define a valid state manifold,
    ensuring that perturbed inputs remain physically plausible and
    consistent with real-world network behaviour, and they restrict the
    optimisation space of PGD, enabling faster convergence and more stealthy
    perturbations.

    \texttt{cur\_br}, the predicted next bitrate, must be one of

    attack (PGD) pipeline as projection bounds to ensure that all adversarial

    % - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - -
    \subsubsection{SpecTRA-Constrained PGD Attack}
    \label{sec:attack-pgd}
    
    We implement a targeted adversarial attack using PGD.  The goal is to
    apply minimal perturbations to the ABR state input in order to force
    the neural policy to select the lowest bitrate ($300$~kbps), thereby
    minimising QoE.
    
    At each step, PGD performs gradient ascent on the targeted loss
    function (cross-entropy with respect to the $300$~kbps class), followed
    by projection back onto a feasible region defined by both an
    $\ell_\infty$-norm budget and the physical bounds mined by SpecTRA.
    To maintain stealth and physical plausibility, we apply PGD to only one
    input feature: \texttt{Last1\_downloadtime}.
    
    The attack proceeds iteratively as shown in
    Algorithm~\ref{alg:pgd-attack}.  In each iteration, the value of
    \texttt{Last1\_throughput} is updated to remain consistent with the
    perturbed \texttt{Last1\_downloadtime}, preserving the relationship
    between throughput and download time and ensuring input realism.
    
    \begin{algorithm}[t]
        \caption{SpecTRA-Constrained PGD Attack}
        \label{alg:pgd-attack}
        \begin{algorithmic}[1]
            \Require Initial state $\mathbf{x}$, model $\pi_\theta$, step
                    size $\alpha$, perturbation bound $\epsilon$,
                    specification bounds $\mathbf{B}_{\text{low}}$,
                    $\mathbf{B}_{\text{high}}$, iterations $T$
            \Ensure  Adversarial state $\tilde{\mathbf{x}}^{(T)}$
            \State Initialise: $\tilde{\mathbf{x}}^{(0)} \gets \mathbf{x}$
            \For{$t = 0$ \textbf{to} $T - 1$}
                \State Compute gradient:
                    $\mathbf{g} \gets \nabla_{\tilde{\mathbf{x}}}
                        \mathcal{L}(\tilde{\mathbf{x}}^{(t)},\, y = 0)$
                \State Gradient step:
                    $\tilde{\mathbf{x}}^{(t+\frac{1}{2})} \gets
                        \tilde{\mathbf{x}}^{(t)} + \alpha \cdot
                        \mathrm{sign}(\mathbf{g})$
                \State Project to $\ell_\infty$ ball:
                    $\tilde{\mathbf{x}}^{(t+\frac{1}{2})} \gets
                        \mathrm{clip}\bigl(
                        \tilde{\mathbf{x}}^{(t+\frac{1}{2})},\;
                        \mathbf{x} - \epsilon,\;
                        \mathbf{x} + \epsilon\bigr)$
                \State Project to SpecTRA bounds:
                    $\tilde{\mathbf{x}}^{(t+1)} \gets
                        \mathrm{clip}\bigl(
                        \tilde{\mathbf{x}}^{(t+\frac{1}{2})},\;
                        \mathbf{B}_{\text{low}},\;
                        \mathbf{B}_{\text{high}}\bigr)$
            \EndFor
            \State \Return $\tilde{\mathbf{x}}^{(T)}$
        \end{algorithmic}
    \end{algorithm}
    
    This attack is simple, fast to compute, and leverages domain
    constraints to ensure that adversarial inputs are realistic and
    plausible under real network conditions.

    \subsection{Evaluation}
    \label{sec:attack-eval}
    
    We evaluate the attack against the Pensieve ABR agent to assess its
    vulnerability to specification-guided adversarial perturbations.
    
    
    % - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - -
    \subsubsection{Experimental Setup}
    \label{sec:attack-setup}
    
    We evaluate the attack using initial values sampled from $30{,}000$ test samples 
    randomly generated against a single SpecTRA-derived specification.  A test sample is considered a \emph{successful attack}
    if the selected bitrate both degrades relative to the unperturbed
    decision and falls outside the nominal output range defined by the
    post-condition.
    
    
    % - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - -
    \subsubsection{Metrics}
    \label{sec:attack-metrics}
    
    We evaluate our attack using three primary metrics:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item \textbf{Attack success rate:} the percentage of states where
            the selected bitrate is both degraded and falls outside the
            nominal output range defined by the specification (i.e.\
            out-of-bound).
        \item \textbf{Average bitrate reduction:} the average decrease in
            selected bitrate across successfully attacked states, compared
            to the original, unperturbed decisions.
        \item \textbf{Average QoE loss:} the mean reduction in QoE
            resulting from the attack.
    \end{itemize}
    These metrics collectively capture both the effectiveness and
    perceptual impact of the attack on the ABR agent's decision-making.

    \subsubsection{Attack Stealth}
    \label{sec:attack-stealth}
    
    All perturbations are limited to a $10\%$ increase in download time,
    which falls within normal network variance and is unlikely to trigger
    anomaly detectors that monitor coarse throughput trends.  Because the
    perturbed states remain inside the SpecTRA-derived valid state manifold,
    they are indistinguishable from natural network fluctuations when
    assessed by standard sanity checks.
    
    
    % ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
    \subsection{Limitations}
    \label{sec:attack-limitations}
    
    The most significant limitation of the attack study is its
    restriction to \emph{two-chunk video runs}: we evaluate QoE over
    a short, fixed horizon without tracking how a suboptimal bitrate
    decision propagates through subsequent rounds.  In practice, ABR
    controllers operate over many chunks, and a single poor action
    can compound through the closed-loop dynamics to produce sustained
    QoE degradation.  This short-horizon scope motivates the
    multi-round verification pipeline developed in \ref{chap:verify},
    which provides exhaustive, formally sound coverage of the
    controller's behaviour across multiple rounds.
    
    
    % ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
    \subsection{Summary}
    \label{sec:attack-summary}
    
    This chapter presented the first systematic study showing that ML-based
    ABR controllers can be compromised by small, in-bound perturbations.
    Using SpecTRA to extract realistic specifications, we embedded those
    constraints in a PGD attack that modifies only one
    feature---\texttt{Last1\_downloadtime}---by at most $10\%$.  This
    lightweight manipulation is sufficient to drive Pensieve to select the
    lowest bitrate in $1.9\%$ of $30{,}000$ test states, reducing average
    bitrate by $35$--$65\%$ and QoE by $30$--$40\%$ across successful
    attacks.  Because the perturbations stay inside an empirically valid
    state manifold, they evade simple sanity checks, exposing a practical
    security risk for neural systems deployments.
    
    These findings motivate the subsequent chapters.  Pensieve's
    vulnerability to small perturbations underscores the need for a
    trustworthy environment model (\ref{chap:env}), and the
    specification-guided attack framework provides the perturbation
    sets and correctness criteria that seed the end-to-end verification
    pipeline (\ref{chap:verify}).

\section{A Statistically Certified Environment Model}
\label{chap:env}

    \ref{chap:attack} demonstrated that Pensieve is vulnerable to
    small, specification-guided perturbations.  Designing principled
    defences requires formal reasoning about the controller's closed-loop
    behaviour, which in turn requires a trustworthy model of the
    environment in which the controller operates.  This chapter constructs
    such a model: a neural predictor $f^{\ast}$ for the environment
    transition function~$f$, equipped with statistically certified error
    bounds.

    Section~\ref{sec:env-prelim} introduces the environment-modelling
    problem and the Puffer dataset.
    Section~\ref{sec:env-architecture} describes the neural network
    architecture and training procedure.
    Section~\ref{sec:env-certification} explains how we derive statistical
    certification guarantees via Clopper--Pearson bounds.
    Section~\ref{sec:env-eval} presents the experimental evaluation.
    Section~\ref{sec:env-summary} summarises the chapter.


    % ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
    \subsection{Preliminaries: Environment Modelling and the Puffer Dataset}
    \label{sec:env-prelim}

    Formal verification of a closed-loop system
    (Equation~\ref{eq:closed-loop}) requires a model of the environment
    transition function~$f$ that maps $(s_t, a_t) \mapsto s_{t+1}$.
    In the ABR setting, $f$ encodes stochastic network dynamics---bandwidth
    fluctuations, TCP congestion-control behaviour, CDN routing---whose
    exact form is unknown and varies across deployments.  While the neural
    controller $\mathrm{NN}$ can be analysed with existing verification
    tools, $f$ cannot: its dynamics must be learned from data and its
    fidelity to the true system must be established empirically.

    The \emph{Puffer} dataset~\cite{fugu} provides a large-scale source of
    real-world streaming telemetry suitable for this purpose.
    Puffer is a live television streaming platform operated by Stanford
    University that logs fine-grained per-chunk statistics---including
    throughput, download time, buffer level, and selected bitrate---for
    every session.
    Because the data is collected from a production system serving real
    users over real networks, it captures the full diversity of network
    conditions that an ABR controller encounters in practice.

    Among the quantities encoded in $f$, next-chunk download time is the
    most consequential for QoE: it governs buffer dynamics and, when
    underestimated, is the proximate cause of rebuffering stalls.  It is
    also the hardest to model, because it is determined by network
    conditions---bandwidth fluctuations, TCP behaviour, CDN routing---that
    are inherently stochastic and not observable in advance.  Existing ABR
    simulators address this by replaying recorded bandwidth traces, but
    they offer no theoretical guarantee that the simulated dynamics
    faithfully represent the true environment; a controller verified in
    such a simulator may behave differently on real networks where
    conditions deviate from the replay traces.  We therefore learn this
    component from the Puffer traces by training a neural network
    $f^{\ast}$ to predict next-chunk download time from a window of recent
    throughput and timing measurements.  Critically, rather than treating
    $f^{\ast}$ as an unverified black box, we derive formal statistical
    guarantees on its prediction accuracy via Clopper--Pearson confidence
    intervals, giving our verification pipeline an correctness
    certificate that trace-replay simulators cannot provide.


    % ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
    \subsection{Environment Model Architecture}
    \label{sec:env-architecture}

    % - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - -
    \subsubsection{Dataset and Features}
    \label{sec:env-data}

    To train the environment predictor $f^{\ast}$, we use real-world ABR
    traces collected from the real-world dataset collected by Puffer~\cite{fugu}.  Each training
    sample represents a state--action pair and contains $19$ features:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item $8$ past bandwidth measurements (in Mbps),
        \item $8$ past download times (in seconds),
        \item $2$ past chunk sizes (in MB),
        \item $1$ upcoming chunk size (in MB).
    \end{itemize}
    These inputs form a $19$-dimensional feature vector used to predict the
    (normalised) next-chunk download time.  This empirical data provides a
    realistic basis for modelling environment transitions in ABR streaming
    under diverse network conditions.


    % - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - -
    \subsubsection{Neural Network Architecture}
    \label{sec:env-nn}

    The environment predictor $f^{\ast}$ is implemented as a fully
    connected neural network.  We experimented with both $2$-hidden-layer
    and $3$-hidden-layer architectures.  The output is a single linear
    regression value corresponding to the predicted next-chunk download
    time.  The network is trained using the Adam optimiser with L1 loss,
    which improves robustness to heavy-tailed download-time fluctuations
    common in real network traces.


    % - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - -
    \subsubsection{Normalisation}
    \label{sec:env-norm}

    All features and labels are normalised to the range $[0, 1]$ by
    dividing by their respective maximum values observed in the training
    set.  Training, evaluation, and certification are performed entirely in
    this normalised space.  This approach ensures numerical stability and
    allows error guarantees to be expressed in terms of relative prediction
    error, consistent with prior work on ABR trace modelling.


    % - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - -
    \subsubsection{Training Procedure}
    \label{sec:env-training}

    We trained two types of models to evaluate generalisation:
    \begin{enumerate}
        \item \textbf{Individual models:} $10$ models, each trained on
            $1{,}000$ samples from a single day and tested on a different
            $1{,}000$ samples from the same day.  These models evaluate
            in-distribution performance.
        \item \textbf{Combined model:} one model trained on $10{,}000$
            samples drawn from $10$ random days ($1{,}000$ per day) and
            tested on $9{,}000$ samples from another $9$ different random
            days.  This model evaluates generalisation across diverse
            network conditions.
    \end{enumerate}

    \subsection{Statistical Certification via Clopper--Pearson Bounds}
    \label{sec:env-certification}

    To provide formal reliability guarantees, we employ Clopper--Pearson
    confidence intervals~\cite{clopper-pearson}, which offer exact binomial
    confidence bounds without parametric assumptions.  The general method is
    reviewed in Section~\ref{sec:bg-cp}; here we describe its concrete
    application to the environment predictor.

    \paragraph{Success criterion.}
    A prediction is considered a \emph{success} if it satisfies
    \begin{equation}
        \lvert f(x) - f^{\ast}(x) \rvert \leq \epsilon,
        \label{eq:env-success}
    \end{equation}
    where $f(x)$ is the ground-truth next-chunk download time,
    $f^{\ast}(x)$ is the predicted value, and $\epsilon = 0.10$ represents
    a $10\%$ tolerance in the normalised domain.  This threshold balances
    precision with practical robustness requirements for ABR systems.

    \paragraph{Confidence interval computation.}
    Given a test set of $n$ samples, suppose $k$ samples satisfy the
    success criterion.  We model each prediction as an independent
    Bernoulli trial with unknown success probability $p$.  Using the
    Clopper--Pearson method (Equation~\ref{eq:cp}), we compute an exact
    $90\%$ confidence interval for $p$ by setting $\alpha = 0.10$.  The
    lower bound of this interval provides our certified guarantee.

    \paragraph{Interpretation.}
    For our best model (the combined model), the $90\%$ Clopper--Pearson
    confidence interval on test-set accuracy is $[0.9846,\, 0.9887]$.  We
    use the lower bound as our certified guarantee:
    \begin{equation}
        \Pr\!\bigl(\lvert f(x) - f^{\ast}(x) \rvert \leq 0.10\bigr)
        \;\geq\; 0.9846
        \quad\text{with $90\%$ confidence.}
        \label{eq:env-certified}
    \end{equation}
    This conservative bound ensures that, with $90\%$ confidence, at least
    $98.46\%$ of future predictions will have less than $10\%$ normalised
    error under the i.i.d.\ assumption.

    \paragraph{Key assumption.}
    The validity of Clopper--Pearson bounds requires that test samples are
    independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.).  We satisfy this
    assumption by: (1)~randomly shuffling trace data before train--test
    splitting, and (2)~ensuring temporal independence by drawing samples
    from different streaming sessions and days.  While real network traces
    exhibit temporal correlations within sessions, our sampling strategy
    mitigates this concern by treating each chunk download as an independent
    observation.

    \paragraph{Providing a certified environment model.}
    The statistical guarantee derived from Clopper--Pearson bounds serves as
    the formal foundation for using $f^{\ast}$ in verification tasks.
    Unlike network simulators deployed without guarantees, our certified
    predictor comes with quantifiable reliability: we know, with high
    confidence, what fraction of predictions will be accurate.  This
    transforms $f^{\ast}$ from a heuristic model into a trustworthy
    symbolic component suitable for reasoning about closed-loop system
    behaviour and enabling principled verification of neural ABR
    controllers.

    \subsubsection{Metrics}

    We evaluate model reliability through statistical certification using
    exact binomial confidence intervals.  Because our goal is a verifiable
    environment model suitable for formal reasoning, we focus on deriving
    provable guarantees on prediction accuracy rather than traditional
    point-wise error metrics.

    A prediction is counted as a \emph{success} if
    $\lvert f(x) - f^{\ast}(x) \rvert \leq 0.10$---that is, the
    prediction error is within $10\%$ in normalised space.  Given the
    observed success rate on a test set, we compute the $90\%$
    Clopper--Pearson confidence interval $[L, U]$ on the true success
    probability.  We report the interval midpoint $(L + U)/2$ with error
    bars of width $(U - L)/2$ to visualise both the point estimate and the
    statistical uncertainty.  The lower bound $L$ provides our certified
    guarantee.

\section{End-to-End QoE Verification Pipeline}
\label{chap:verify}

    The previous two chapters established the building blocks for formal
    reasoning about Pensieve's closed-loop behaviour: a concrete threat
    model demonstrating adversarial vulnerability
    (\ref{chap:attack}) and a statistically certified environment
    predictor $f^{\ast}$ (\ref{chap:env}).  This chapter unifies
    them into an end-to-end verification pipeline that reasons about
    multi-round QoE properties of the complete neurosymbolic control loop.

    The pipeline alternates between two phases at each control round~$t$:
    \begin{enumerate}
        \item \textbf{Pensieve phase.}  Given a bounded input region
            $[\ell_t, u_t]$, we use auto\_LiRPA~\cite{auto-lirpa}
            to compute output bounds for the Pensieve controller.  Where
            the bounds are too loose to verify a single dominant action,
            we split the input region into smaller subregions and re-verify
            each one.  Subregions where a single action dominates are
            directly certified; subregions that remain ambiguous at the
            maximum split depth are \emph{forked}---the same state bounds
            are paired with each remaining candidate action, creating
            one branch per action in the reachability tree.
        \item \textbf{Environment phase.}  For each subregion--action pair,
            we compose the subregion bounds with
            $f^{\ast}$ and propagate interval bounds through the
            environment predictor using LiRPA.  This yields guaranteed
            next-state bounds $[\ell_{t+1}, u_{t+1}]$ that become the
            input region for round $t{+}1$.
    \end{enumerate}
    Iterating these two phases for $T$ rounds produces a \emph{hierarchical
    reachability tree} (Section~\ref{sec:reachability-tree}), where each path
    from root to leaf represents a verified action sequence with
    guaranteed QoE bounds.  Figure~\ref{fig:pipeline-overview} illustrates
    this two-phase structure.

    \begin{figure}[th]
        \centering
        \includegraphics[width=0.95\linewidth]{figures/pipeline_overview_src.pdf}
        \caption{Overview of the two-phase verification pipeline.  At each
            round~$t$, the Pensieve phase applies LiRPA bound propagation
            and region splitting to produce verified actions~$a_t$, and
            the environment phase propagates bounds through the certified
            predictor $f^{\ast}$ to yield next-state bounds
            $[\ell_{t+1}, u_{t+1}]$.  Iterating across rounds builds a
            hierarchical reachability tree whose root-to-leaf paths are
            verified action sequences with guaranteed $\Delta$QoE bounds.}
        \label{fig:pipeline-overview}
    \end{figure}

    Section~\ref{sec:verify-formulation} formulates the ABR pipeline as a
    neurosymbolic system and defines the verification target.
    Section~\ref{sec:verify-pensieve-phase} describes the Pensieve
    verification phase, including our input-region splitting strategy.
    Section~\ref{sec:verify-env-phase} describes the environment
    verification phase.
    Section~\ref{sec:reachability-tree} explains how multi-round
    composition of both phases produces a hierarchical reachability tree.
    Section~\ref{sec:verify-impl} describes the implementation optimisations
    that make the pipeline tractable in practice.
    Section~\ref{sec:verify-theory} presents theoretical soundness
    guarantees and the statistical compounding bound.
    Section~\ref{sec:verify-results} presents the reachability results.
    Section~\ref{sec:verify-summary} summarises the chapter.


    % ===========================================================================
    \subsection{Neurosymbolic System Formulation}
    \label{sec:verify-formulation}

    We model ABR streaming as a neurosymbolic closed-loop system composed
    of two interacting components: (1)~a \emph{neural controller} that
    selects bitrates based on the current state, and (2)~an
    \emph{environment transition function} that determines how each bitrate
    decision influences future network and playback behaviour.  This
    decomposition captures both the expressive, data-driven decision logic
    of the controller and the structured evolution of the underlying system
    state.

    \paragraph{System dynamics.}
    Let $s_t$ denote the system state at time~$t$, consisting of recent
    throughput measurements, download times, buffer occupancy, past
    selected bitrates, available chunk sizes, and remaining chunks.  The neural
    ABR controller selects a bitrate $a_t = \mathrm{NN}(s_t)$ based on this
    state.  The environment transition function~$f$ then maps
    $(s_t, a_t)$ to the next state:
    \begin{equation}
        s_{t+1} = f(s_t,\, a_t).
        \label{eq:verify-transition}
    \end{equation}
    The function $f$ embodies complex network characteristics---bandwidth
    fluctuations, network delay, chunk sizes---whose exact form is unknown.
    While we can observe state transitions in real traces, we cannot
    directly access or verify $f$ itself.  The certified predictor
    $f^{\ast}$ constructed in \ref{chap:env} serves as our
    trustworthy approximation.

    \paragraph{$\Delta$QoE metric.}
    Following the same rebuffering exclusion as in
    Section~\ref{sec:attack-simplification} ($\mu = 0$), we define the
    \emph{marginal QoE contribution} at step~$t$ as
    \begin{equation}
        \Delta\mathrm{QoE}_t
        \;=\; q(a_t) \;-\; \lvert q(a_t) - q(a_{t-1}) \rvert,
        \label{eq:delta-qoe}
    \end{equation}
    where $q(a_t)$ is the bitrate utility of the selected action
    (Equation~\ref{eq:bg-utility}) and the second term penalises
    quality switches between consecutive chunks ($\nu = 1$).
    A positive value indicates that the selected bitrate contributes
    positively to the session QoE; a negative value indicates that
    the quality-switch penalty exceeds the bitrate utility.

    \paragraph{Verification target.}
    Algorithm~\ref{alg:neurosymbolic} formalises the closed-loop
    interaction between the neural controller and the certified environment
    model.  The system tracks consecutive rounds in which
    $\Delta\text{QoE}$ falls below a minimum acceptable
    threshold~$\alpha$.  The assertion at line~15 checks that either
    the per-step QoE contribution is acceptable
    (i.e.\ $\Delta\text{QoE} \geq \alpha$), or the number of
    consecutive low-$\Delta\text{QoE}$ rounds stays below a tolerance limit~$\beta$.
    This $\Delta\text{QoE}$ assertion detects sustained deterioration
    even when individual actions select high bitrates, while still
    accommodating transient quality switches under network conditions.

    \begin{algorithm}[t]
        \caption{Neurosymbolic formulation of ABR streaming}
        \label{alg:neurosymbolic}
        \begin{algorithmic}[1]
            \Require Initial state $s_0$, Pensieve controller $\mathrm{NN}$,
                    certified predictor $f^{\ast}$, $\Delta$QoE threshold $\alpha$,
                    violation tolerance $\beta$
            \State $a_{\text{prev}} \gets \bot$
                \Comment{No previous action at start}
            \State $a \gets \mathrm{NN}(s_0)$
                \Comment{Initial action from Pensieve}
            \State $s \gets f^{\ast}(s_0,\, a)$
                \Comment{Environment transition via certified predictor}
            \State $\mathit{failure\_counter} \gets 0$
            \While{\textbf{true}}
                \State $a_{\text{prev}} \gets a$
                \State $a \gets \mathrm{NN}(s)$
                    \Comment{Pensieve outputs next bitrate decision}
                \State $s \gets f^{\ast}(s,\, a)$
                    \Comment{Update state using certified environment model}
                \State $\Delta\text{QoE} \gets \text{QoE}(a,\, a_{\text{prev}})$
                    \Comment{Marginal QoE contribution (Equation~\ref{eq:delta-qoe})}
                \If{$\Delta\text{QoE} < \alpha$}
                    \State $\mathit{failure\_counter} \gets
                        \mathit{failure\_counter} + 1$
                \Else
                    \State $\mathit{failure\_counter} \gets 0$
                \EndIf
                \State \textbf{assert}
                    $(\Delta\text{QoE} \geq \alpha) \;\lor\;
                        (\mathit{failure\_counter} < \beta)$
            \EndWhile
        \end{algorithmic}
    \end{algorithm}

    The verification goal is to determine, for a given initial state
    region, whether the $\Delta\text{QoE}$ assertion in
    Algorithm~\ref{alg:neurosymbolic} holds across multiple control
    rounds, or to identify concrete action sequences that lead to
    sustained QoE degradation.


    % ===========================================================================
    \subsection{Pensieve Verification Phase}
    \label{sec:verify-pensieve-phase}

    The Pensieve phase (lines~7 of
    Algorithm~\ref{alg:neurosymbolic}) takes a bounded input region
    $[\ell, u] \subseteq \mathbb{R}^{48}$, where
    $\ell, u \in \mathbb{R}^{48}$ are
    component-wise lower and upper bounds on the full state vector
    $s = (s^{(1)}, \ldots, s^{(48)})$, encompassing all six input groups:
    past throughputs, download times, buffer occupancy, past bitrates,
    available chunk sizes, and remaining chunks
    (Section~\ref{subsec:streaming}).  The phase determines which of
    Pensieve's six bitrate actions is provably dominant over the entire
    region.  We first compute output bounds using
    LiRPA (Section~\ref{sec:bg-lirpa}), then apply input-region splitting
    when the initial bounds are insufficient.


    % ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
    \subsubsection{Bound Computation via LiRPA}
    \label{sec:verify-lirpa-usage}

    Given the input region $[\ell, u]$, we invoke
    auto\_LiRPA~\cite{auto-lirpa} with CROWN-Optimised bound
    propagation (Section~\ref{sec:bg-lirpa}) to compute lower and upper
    bounds $[\underline{y}_i, \overline{y}_i]$ for each output logit $y_i$,
    $i \in \{0, \ldots, 5\}$.

    An action $a_j$ is \emph{provably dominant} over $[\ell, u]$ if its
    lower bound exceeds the upper bounds of all other actions:
    \begin{equation}
        \underline{y}_j > \overline{y}_i
        \quad \forall\, i \neq j.
        \label{eq:dominant}
    \end{equation}
    When this condition holds, region $[\ell, u]$ is \emph{certified} for
    action~$a_j$: every input in the region is guaranteed to produce the
    same controller decision.

    If no single action satisfies Equation~\ref{eq:dominant}, the output
    bounds are too loose---the relaxation introduced by LiRPA leaves
    ambiguity about which action Pensieve would select.  In this case, we
    refine the analysis by splitting the input region.


    % ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
    \subsubsection{Input-Region Splitting}
    \label{sec:verify-splitting}

    When LiRPA cannot verify a dominant action for the full input region,
    the output bounds for multiple actions overlap: no single action
    satisfies the dominance condition (Equation~\ref{eq:dominant}), so
    $\Delta\mathrm{QoE}$ cannot be attributed to a single, known action.
    We resolve this ambiguity by bisecting the input region along the
    throughput dimension and re-verifying each half independently.
    Smaller subregions yield tighter LiRPA bounds, which can separate
    previously overlapping actions.  Figure~\ref{fig:splitting} illustrates
    the two outcomes: a subregion whose bounds are now tight enough to
    verify a single dominant action produces a clean $\Delta\mathrm{QoE}$
    value; a subregion that remains ambiguous after exhausting the split
    budget is handled by propagating the same state bounds forward
    independently for each candidate action.

    \begin{figure}[t]
        \centering
        \includegraphics[width=0.98\linewidth]{figures/bound_splitting_src.pdf}
        \caption{Input-region splitting for action certification and
            $\Delta\mathrm{QoE}$ computation.
            \textbf{(a)}~For the full region $[\ell, u]$, LiRPA output
            bounds for $a_3$ and $a_4$ overlap: the dominant action
            is ambiguous, so $\Delta\mathrm{QoE}$ cannot be determined.
            The region is bisected along the most recent throughput
            dimension, producing $[\ell_1, u_1]$ and $[\ell_2, u_2]$
            that differ only in that single component.
            \textbf{(b)}~In $[\ell_1, u_1]$ the tighter bounds certify
            $a_3$ as uniquely dominant, yielding a single
            $\Delta\mathrm{QoE}$ value for that subregion.
            \textbf{(c)}~In $[\ell_2, u_2]$ ambiguity persists after
            the split budget is exhausted; the same state bounds are
            propagated forward separately for each candidate action
            ($a_3$ and $a_4$), creating two branches in the
            reachability tree.}
        \label{fig:splitting}
    \end{figure}

    \paragraph{Splitting strategy.}
    Our splitting procedure operates as follows.  Given an input region
    $[\ell, u]$ for which no dominant action can be certified:
    \begin{enumerate}
        \item \textbf{Throughput bisection.}  Let $d$ denote the index
            of the most recent throughput in the $48$-dimensional state
            vector (i.e.\ the last entry in the throughput group).  We
            bisect the region along dimension~$d$ only, computing the
            midpoint
            \begin{equation}
                m = \bigl(\ell^{(d)} + u^{(d)}\bigr) / 2,
                \label{eq:split-dim}
            \end{equation}
            and producing two subregions $[\ell_1, u_1]$ and
            $[\ell_2, u_2]$ that are identical to $[\ell, u]$ in all
            $47$ other dimensions but differ in the throughput
            component: $u_1^{(d)} = m$ and $\ell_2^{(d)} = m$.
            We always split on this single dimension because throughput
            is derived from chunk size divided by chunk download time,
            making it consistently the widest dimension in the input
            region.
        \item \textbf{Re-verification.}  Each subregion is independently
            verified using LiRPA.  If a subregion admits a dominant
            action, it is verified as a subregion--action pair with a
            $\Delta\mathrm{QoE}$ value.  Otherwise, it is placed back in
            the queue for further splitting.
        \item \textbf{Termination and forking.}  The procedure terminates
            when all subregions are certified or a maximum split
            depth~$D$ is reached (we use $D = 8$).  Subregions that
            remain ambiguous at depth~$D$ are not discarded; instead,
            the same state bounds are propagated forward once per
            candidate action, creating parallel branches in the
            reachability tree and ensuring no reachable state is
            omitted.
    \end{enumerate}

    Classical branch-and-bound (BaB) methods in complete neural network
    verification~\cite{bab} also use splitting to tighten bounds,
    on either intermediate neurons or input dimensions.  Our approach
    differs in two key respects.  First, we split exclusively on a
    single, \emph{physically meaningful} input dimension (the most
    recent throughput), so that each subregion corresponds to a
    contiguous region of the ABR state space and can be fed directly
    into the environment phase as a self-contained input.  Second,
    our goal is not to prove a single binary property but to
    \emph{partition} the input space into verified regions, each
    paired with an single action, building the foundation for multi-round
    QoE analysis.


    % ===========================================================================
    \subsection{Environment Verification Phase}
    \label{sec:verify-env-phase}

    For each subregion--action pair $([\ell, u],\, a_j)$ produced by the
    Pensieve phase, the environment phase (line~8 of
    Algorithm~\ref{alg:neurosymbolic}) computes guaranteed next-state
    bounds.  The inputs to $f^{\ast}$ are constructed by combining the
    state bounds $[\ell, u]$ with the action~$a_j$.  We then
    propagate these bounds through the environment predictor $f^{\ast}$
    (\ref{chap:env}) using LiRPA (Section~\ref{sec:bg-lirpa}),
    obtaining
    \begin{equation}
        [\ell_{t+1},\, u_{t+1}]
        \supseteq \bigl\{f^{\ast}(s, a_j) : s \in [\ell, u]\bigr\}.
        \label{eq:env-bounds}
    \end{equation}
    The over-approximation is sound: the true next state for any
    $s \in [\ell, u]$ under action $a_j$ is guaranteed to lie within
    $[\ell_{t+1}, u_{t+1}]$.  The resulting next-state bounds serve as the
    input region for the Pensieve phase of the subsequent round.  Because
    the environment phase introduces its own over-approximation (from LiRPA
    relaxation of $f^{\ast}$), and because $f^{\ast}$ is itself an
    approximation of the true dynamics~$f$ (with certified accuracy from
    \ref{chap:env}), the next-state bounds grow with each round.
    Section~\ref{sec:verify-theory} formalises how these two sources of
    imprecision compound across rounds.


    % ===========================================================================
    \subsection{Multi-Round Reachability Analysis}
    \label{sec:reachability-tree}

    Composing the Pensieve and environment phases across $T$ rounds
    produces a \emph{hierarchical reachability tree}.

    \paragraph{Tree structure.}
    The root node represents the initial input region $[\ell_0, u_0]$,
    typically derived from a SpecTRA specification
    (Section~\ref{sec:bg-spectra}).  At each Pensieve-phase level,
    the region is split into subregions, each paired with a certified
    action (a single subregion may appear multiple times if it was forked
    across candidate actions).  At each environment-phase level,
    each subregion--action pair produces a next-state bound via
    $f^{\ast}$.  The children of an environment node are the
    subregion--action pairs produced by the next Pensieve phase applied
    to that next-state bound.

    \paragraph{Action sequences.}
    Each root-to-leaf path through the tree defines a \emph{certified
    action sequence}: a series of bitrate decisions
    $a_0 \to a_1 \to \cdots \to a_{T-1}$ for which every intermediate
    state is guaranteed to lie within computed bounds.  The set of all such
    paths enumerates the possible behaviours of the controller over $T$
    rounds, subject to the over-approximation introduced by LiRPA and the
    statistical certification of $f^{\ast}$.

    \paragraph{QoE analysis.}
    For each verified action sequence, we compute the $\Delta$QoE
    (Equation~\ref{eq:delta-qoe}) at every action transition using the
    logarithmic utility $q(R) = \ln(R/R_{\min})$
    (Equation~\eqref{eq:utility}).
    Figure~\ref{fig:delta-qoe-tree} illustrates this
    propagation for the two-round Specification~5 tree: the initial
    action selection at round~0 determines whether the cumulative QoE
    is low ($a_1$ path, cumulative $+0.50$) or high ($a_2$ path,
    cumulative $+1.91$), even though both paths converge to the same
    action at round~1.
    A sequence is flagged as potentially problematic if the $\Delta$QoE
    is negative at any round, indicating that the quality-switch
    penalty exceeds the bitrate utility of the selected action.


    % ===========================================================================
    \subsection{Implementation and Optimisations}
    \label{sec:verify-impl}

    The verification pipeline as described is computationally demanding:
    each bound-propagation pass carries significant overhead from
    CROWN-Optimised relaxation, and the branching structure of the
    reachability tree can produce thousands of subregions per round.  Three
    targeted optimisations make the pipeline tractable in practice:
    a single-pass \textsc{alldominance} layer that eliminates redundant bound
    propagation calls (Section~\ref{sec:verify-alldominance}), batched
    verification that amortises per-call overhead across many subregions
    (Section~\ref{sec:verify-batching}), and multi-GPU parallelism that
    processes independent regions concurrently across four H100 devices
    (Section~\ref{sec:verify-multigpu}).


    % ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
    \subsubsection{Single-Pass Dominance via \textsc{alldominance}}
    \label{sec:verify-alldominance}

    A naive implementation of the dominance check
    (Equation~\ref{eq:dominant}) requires one bound-propagation pass per
    candidate action: for each $a_j$, bounds are propagated through the
    network and checked against the remaining actions.  For Pensieve's six
    actions this incurs six separate passes per region.

    We eliminate this redundancy by appending a fixed linear layer to
    the Pensieve neural network.  We define a \emph{dominance matrix}
    $M \in \mathbb{R}^{30 \times 6}$, where each of the
    $6 \times 5 = 30$ rows encodes the pairwise logit difference $y_k - y_i$
    for a distinct ordered pair $(k,i)$ with $k \neq i$.  The composite model
    \begin{equation}
        g(x) = M \cdot \mathrm{NN}(x)
        \label{eq:alldominance}
    \end{equation}
    produces a $30$-dimensional output in a single forward pass.  Because $M$
    is a constant matrix with no trainable parameters, LiRPA propagates
    bounds through it analytically with negligible overhead.  The dominance
    check then reduces to a single comparison against the lower-bound
    vector $\underline{g}(x) \in \mathbb{R}^{30}$:
    \begin{multline}
        \text{action } a_k \text{ is verified dominant}
        \Longleftarrow \\
        \underline{g}(x)_{j} \geq 0
        \quad \forall\, j \in \bigl\{k(a_{\dim}{-}1),\, \ldots,\,
        (k{+}1)(a_{\dim}{-}1) - 1\bigr\},
        \label{eq:alldominance-check}
    \end{multline}
    where $a_{\dim} = 6$.  All six dominance decisions are therefore resolved
    in a single bound-propagation pass---a $6\times$ reduction in the
    dominant computational cost of the inner verification loop.


    % ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
    \subsubsection{Batched Bound Propagation}
    \label{sec:verify-batching}

    CROWN-Optimised bound propagation incurs a fixed per-call overhead
    (coefficient optimisation, graph traversal, GPU kernel launch) that is
    amortised across all elements in the batch.  Processing subregions one
    at a time therefore wastes the vast majority of available GPU
    throughput.

    We exploit the batch dimension of auto\_LiRPA: given $B$
    subregions with bounds $(\ell^{(1)}, u^{(1)}), \ldots,
    (\ell^{(B)}, u^{(B)})$, each of shape $1 \times 6 \times 8$, we stack
    them along the batch axis to form a single $B \times 6 \times 8$
    input tensor and issue one bound-propagation call returning a
    $B \times 30$ lower-bound tensor.  Dominance is then checked
    independently for each element of the batch.
    We cap the batch size at $B = 256$ subregions per call.  Even at this
    size, GPU utilisation remains modest (approximately $10\%$ compute,
    $20\%$ memory on an H100), indicating that the bottleneck lies in the
    per-call overhead of bound propagation rather than raw arithmetic
    throughput.  For a queue of $N$ pending subregions, the
    total number of bound-propagation calls is therefore
    $\lceil N / 256 \rceil$ rather than $N$.


    % ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
    \subsubsection{Multi-GPU Parallelism}
    \label{sec:verify-multigpu}

    The Pensieve and environment phases for distinct regions within a given
    round are mutually independent: no region's verification result depends
    on another's.  We exploit this structure by distributing regions across
    four NVIDIA H100 GPUs.

    Three worker threads, each pinned to a dedicated GPU, own independent
    copies of the LiRPA model instances initialised once at thread
    start-up.  A fourth GPU is reserved for the main thread's lightweight
    logging passes---a single forward call per round to record logit
    bounds for the verification report.  Because all model copies are
    thread-local, there is zero inter-thread synchronisation during bound
    propagation: each worker processes its assigned regions entirely on
    its own device.

    At each round the main thread partitions the current region list into
    per-worker tasks, dispatches them in parallel, and collects results.
    After all workers return, the main thread renumbers the workers' local
    node identifiers to globally unique IDs before logging the completed tree level
    and dispatching the next round.  This multi-threaded design avoids
    inter-process serialisation overhead while allowing concurrent GPU
    execution.

    \subsection{Theoretical Guarantees}
    \label{sec:verify-theory}


    % ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
    \subsubsection{Soundness}
    \label{sec:verify-soundness}

    The verification pipeline inherits the soundness of its two components.

    \begin{theorem}[Pensieve-phase soundness]
    \label{thm:pensieve-sound}
    If LiRPA verifies action $a_j$ as dominant over region $[\ell, u]$
    (Equation~\ref{eq:dominant}), then for every $s \in [\ell, u]$,
    $\arg\max_i \mathrm{NN}(s)_i = j$.
    \end{theorem}

    \begin{proof}
    By the soundness of LiRPA (Section~\ref{sec:bg-lirpa}), for every
    $s \in [\ell, u]$ and every output index~$i$, the true logit satisfies
    $\underline{y}_i \leq \mathrm{NN}(s)_i \leq \overline{y}_i$.  If
    $\underline{y}_j > \overline{y}_i$ for all $i \neq j$, then
    $\mathrm{NN}(s)_j > \mathrm{NN}(s)_i$ for all $i \neq j$, so action
    $a_j$ is selected.
    \end{proof}

    \begin{theorem}[Environment-phase soundness]
    \label{thm:env-sound}
    If LiRPA computes next-state bounds $[\ell_{t+1}, u_{t+1}]$ by
    propagating $[\ell_t, u_t]$ and action $a_j$ through $f^{\ast}$, then
    for every $s \in [\ell_t, u_t]$,
    $f^{\ast}(s, a_j) \in [\ell_{t+1}, u_{t+1}]$.
    \end{theorem}

    \begin{proof}
    This follows directly from the soundness of LiRPA applied to
    $f^{\ast}$, which is a neural network with known architecture and
    weights.
    \end{proof}

    \begin{corollary}[Pipeline soundness]
    \label{cor:pipeline-sound}
    The set of verified action sequences in the reachability tree is a
    sound over-approximation of all action sequences that the real
    system $s_{t+1} = f(s_t, a_t)$, $a_t = \mathrm{NN}(s_t)$ can
    produce for any $s_0 \in [\ell_0, u_0]$, provided that
    $f^{\ast}$ approximates $f$ within its certified error bound at
    every round.  That is, every action sequence realizable by the
    system is contained in the tree, though the tree may also include
    spurious sequences due to bound looseness.
    \end{corollary}

    \begin{proof}
    By induction on the number of rounds.  The base case ($T = 1$) is
    Theorem~\ref{thm:pensieve-sound}.  For the inductive step, suppose
    all states at round~$t$ lie within $[\ell_t, u_t]$.  By
    Theorem~\ref{thm:pensieve-sound}, the verified action $a_t$ is
    selected for every such state.  By Theorem~\ref{thm:env-sound},
    $f^{\ast}(s_t, a_t) \in [\ell_{t+1}, u_{t+1}]$, and since
    $f^{\ast}$ is within its certified error bound, the true next
    state $f(s_t, a_t)$ also lies within $[\ell_{t+1}, u_{t+1}]$.
    The invariant thus holds at round $t{+}1$.
    \end{proof}

    The condition that $f^{\ast}$ stays within its error bound holds
    with probability at least $p^T$, quantified by the statistical
    compounding bound in the following subsection.


    % ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
    \subsubsection{Statistical Compounding Bound}
    \label{sec:verify-compounding}

    The certified predictor $f^{\ast}$ satisfies
    $\Pr(\lvert f(x) - f^{\ast}(x) \rvert \leq \epsilon) \geq p$
    with confidence $1 - \delta$ (\ref{chap:env}), where
    $p = 0.9846$ and $\epsilon = 0.10$ in our experiments.  Over $T$
    rounds, the probability that \emph{all} environment predictions remain
    within tolerance is bounded by:

    \begin{theorem}[Compounding bound]
    \label{thm:compounding}
    Under the i.i.d.\ assumption on successive environment predictions,
    the probability that every round's prediction incurs less than
    $\epsilon$ normalised error satisfies
    \begin{equation}
        \Pr\!\Bigl(\bigcap_{t=1}^{T}
            \lvert f(x_t) - f^{\ast}(x_t) \rvert \leq \epsilon\Bigr)
        \;\geq\; p^T.
        \label{eq:compounding}
    \end{equation}
    \end{theorem}

    \begin{proof}
    Each round's prediction is modelled as an independent Bernoulli trial
    with success probability at least $p$.  The probability that all $T$
    trials succeed is at least $p^T$.
    \end{proof}

    For $T = 5$ rounds and $p = 0.9846$, the compounding bound gives
    $p^5 \geq 0.9246$: with $90\%$ confidence, at least $92.46\%$ of
    five-round trajectories have all environment predictions within
    $10\%$ normalised error.

    \paragraph{Interpretation.}
    The compounding bound quantifies how the irreducible gap between
    $f^{\ast}$ and $f$ accumulates across rounds.  It does \emph{not}
    account for the LiRPA over-approximation error (which is deterministic
    and captured by the interval widths), but rather the statistical
    uncertainty about whether $f^{\ast}$ faithfully represents the true
    dynamics at each step.  The two sources of imprecision are
    complementary: LiRPA bounds grow geometrically with rounds (a
    deterministic effect), while the compounding bound degrades
    multiplicatively (a statistical effect).

    \paragraph{Limitations of the i.i.d.\ assumption.}
    The independence assumption in Theorem~\ref{thm:compounding} is an
    idealisation.  In practice, successive states are correlated because
    each state is derived from the previous one via $f^{\ast}$.  The bound
    $p^T$ is therefore a first-order approximation.  Tighter bounds could be
    obtained by modelling the Markovian structure of the state sequence, but
    this is left to future work.

    degradation.

    Specification~3 stands out with three distinct actions at both
    round~0 and round~3, reflecting the widest initial throughput range
    $[0.85,\, 11.53]$ among all specifications.  The breadth of the
    verified action set at a given round directly measures the
    controller's sensitivity to state perturbations within that
    region.

    for Specification~5 using the logarithmic utility
    $q(R) = \ln(R/R_{\min})$: subregions assigned $a_1$ at round~0
    suffer a $\Delta$QoE of $-0.42$, while those assigned $a_2$
    achieve $+0.52$.  Although both paths recover at round~1 (all
    subregions resolve to~$a_4$), the cumulative QoE diverges: $+0.50$
    for the $a_1$ path versus $+1.91$ for the $a_2$ path.  This
    $1.41$-point gap is determined entirely by the initial action
    selection, illustrating how a single suboptimal decision can
    dominate the multi-round QoE outcome.

    Across all five specifications, the most severe QoE degradation
    occurs in Specification~1, where the sequence
    $a_2 \to a_5 \to a_2$ produces two large quality-switch penalties
    in successive rounds: a $3100$~kbps increase from $a_2$ to~$a_5$
    at round~2, followed by a $3100$~kbps decrease at round~3.
    In contrast, Specification~4 follows a monotonic path
    $a_1, a_2 \to a_4 \to a_5 \to a_5$, where quality only increases
    and then stabilises---the best-case QoE trajectory among all five
    specifications.

    These findings are consistent with the adversarial vulnerability
    established in \ref{chap:attack}: Pensieve is sensitive to
    small state perturbations that shift the selected action across
    bitrate levels.  The reachability tree makes this sensitivity
    \emph{exhaustive}: rather than sampling individual adversarial
    examples, the pipeline certifies all possible behaviours within
    each specification region.  Where \ref{chap:attack} identifies
    that vulnerabilities \emph{exist}, the verification pipeline
    characterises \emph{which} subregions are vulnerable and
    \emph{how} the resulting action sequences degrade QoE over
    multiple rounds.

    \paragraph{Statistical compounding.}
    Applying the compounding bound (Theorem~\ref{thm:compounding}) with
    $p = 0.9846$ and $T = 5$, the QoE guarantees from the reachability
    tree hold for at least $92.46\%$ of real trajectories ($90\%$
    confidence), assuming the i.i.d.\ condition in
    Section~\ref{sec:verify-compounding}.  The remaining $7.54\%$ may
    violate the environment model's error bound, in which case the
    verified action sequences are no longer guaranteed, although the
    Pensieve-phase bounds remain sound regardless.

    \label{sec:verify-summary}

    This chapter presented an end-to-end verification pipeline alternating
    between a Pensieve phase (LiRPA bound propagation with input-region
    splitting) and an environment phase ($f^{\ast}$ interval propagation)
    to build hierarchical reachability trees with $\Delta$QoE annotations.
    We established formal soundness guarantees
    (Theorems~\ref{thm:pensieve-sound} and~\ref{thm:env-sound}) and a

    the three contributions introduced in \ref{chap:intro}.

\section{Discussion}
\label{chap:discussion}

    This chapter discusses how the adversarial attack and verification
    approaches reinforce each other, identifies limitations, and
    outlines future directions.


    % ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
    \subsection{Attack--Verification Synergy}
    \label{sec:disc-synergy}

    The adversarial attack (\ref{chap:attack}) and the verification
    pipeline (\ref{chap:verify}) address the same question, how does
    Pensieve behave under worst-case inputs, complementarily.  
    The attack is \emph{targeted} and \emph{incomplete}: it
    finds specific inputs that cause degradation without exhaustive coverage.  
    The verification is \emph{exhaustive} and \emph{sound}: it covers all 
    possible behaviours within a region with over-approximation and computational cost.

    The two reinforce each other.  The attack identifies which
    specifications contain vulnerable states, guiding verification
    toward regions where formal analysis is most valuable.  Conversely,
    the reachability tree validates attack findings: if an adversarial
    action sequence appears in the tree, the vulnerability is
    confirmed; if not, the attack may have exploited states outside the
    verified region.  The experimental results bear this out: the
    attack found $581$ adversarial examples near action boundaries,
    and the verification pipeline reveals the same sensitivity
    structurally, with specifications spanning wider throughput ranges
    exhibiting more action branching.


    % ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
    \subsection{Limitations}
    \label{sec:disc-limitations}

    \paragraph{QoE completeness.}
    The QoE metric omits the rebuffering penalty ($\mu = 0$) because
    the certified environment predictor produces bounded next-state
    estimates rather than actual download times.  Rebuffering depends on
    whether the selected bitrate exceeds the available throughput for
    long enough to drain the playback buffer, a condition that requires
    tight bounds on both throughput \emph{and} buffer occupancy over
    time.  Our environment predictor bounds only the next-state
    throughput; propagating those bounds through a buffer dynamics model
    would compound the over-approximation at each step, yielding
    rebuffering intervals so wide as to be uninformative.
    Since rebuffering is often the dominant factor in perceived quality,
    the reported $\Delta$QoE values represent an \emph{optimistic} view
    of system performance: the true QoE may be lower in regions where
    the controller selects a high bitrate that the network cannot
    sustain.

    \paragraph{Scalability.}
    The reachability tree grows exponentially with both the number of
    rounds and the degree of action ambiguity at each round.  Five
    rounds are tractable on four NVIDIA H100 GPUs, but each additional
    round multiplies the frontier by the number of candidate actions per
    subregion---in Specification~3, it produces hundreds of leaf nodes by round~4.  Longer horizons would
    require either convergence-based pruning~\cite{pruning} or substantially more computation.
    The five-round horizon is nonetheless sufficient to reveal the key
    behavioural patterns: oscillatory degradation, persistent branching,
    and monotonic convergence all emerge within the first three to four
    rounds.

    \paragraph{Assertion thresholds.}
    Algorithm~\ref{alg:neurosymbolic} parameterises the verification
    target with a $\Delta$QoE threshold $\alpha$ and a consecutive
    violation tolerance $\beta$, which we do not instantiate with
    specific values.  The appropriate thresholds depend on
    deployment context: a live-television service may tolerate no
    negative $\Delta$QoE ($\alpha = 0$, $\beta = 1$), while a
    best-effort mobile player might accept moderate switches
    ($\alpha = -1.0$).  Rather than choosing values that would anchor
    the analysis to one operating point, we report raw $\Delta$QoE for
    all verified sequences, enabling operators to apply their own
    policy post hoc.

    \paragraph{Single controller and dataset.}
    The pipeline is evaluated on Pensieve---a feed-forward network with
    a discrete action set---trained and tested on Puffer
    traces~\cite{fugu}.  Extending to controllers with different
    architectures (e.g.\ Fugu's hybrid MPC design) would require
    adapting the Pensieve verification phase; Fugu's MPC component
    introduces a discrete optimisation step outside the scope of
    differentiable bound propagation.  Similarly, the environment
    predictor is trained on a single dataset; its certified accuracy
    may not transfer to networks with substantially different traffic
    characteristics.  The SpecTRA specifications are likewise derived
    from Pensieve's training traces and cover only a subset of the
    reachable state space.


    % ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
    \subsection{Future Directions}
    \label{sec:disc-future}

    Several directions could strengthen the framework.
    Integrating complete methods (MILP~\cite{bab}, neuron-level
    branch-and-bound~\cite{abcrown}) into the Pensieve phase would
    reduce spurious action branching, and modelling Markovian
    correlation~\cite{markov} between successive states would tighten
    the compounding bound beyond the i.i.d.\ assumption.  A certified
    download-time model could incorporate rebuffering into the QoE
    metric, closing the largest gap with the standard Pensieve objective.
    The reachability tree could also guide targeted testing: for action
    sequences with poor $\Delta$QoE, concrete states can be sampled from
    the verified bounds and executed through the real system to confirm
    whether the degradation is genuine or an artefact of
    over-approximation.  More broadly, reachability trees that expose
    poor worst-case trajectories could inform verification-guided
    retraining, producing neurosymbolic ABR controllers that are
    verifiably safe by construction.

\section{Related Work}
\label{chap:related}

    This chapter surveys related work across the areas that intersect
    in this thesis: neural network verification, adversarial robustness
    in control systems, adaptive bitrate streaming, and formal methods
    for networked systems.


    % ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
    \subsection{Neural Network Verification}
    \label{sec:related-nnv}

    Formal verification of neural networks originated with
    Reluplex~\cite{reluplex}, an SMT-based solver for networks with
    ReLU activations, and its successor Marabou~\cite{marabou}, which
    added support for larger architectures and richer property types.
    These \emph{complete} methods can prove or disprove a property
    exactly but suffer from exponential worst-case complexity.
    MILP formulations offer an alternative complete approach,
    encoding ReLU networks as linear programs with integer constraints;
    branch-and-bound algorithms improve tractability by lazily
    splitting ReLU states.

    \emph{Incomplete} (but sound) methods trade precision for
    scalability.  Bound-propagation methods such as
    CROWN~\cite{crown} compute linear relaxations of the network,
    producing sound output bounds in polynomial time.  The optimised
    variant $\alpha$,$\beta$-CROWN~\cite{abcrown} combines
    bound propagation with branch-and-bound and has dominated the
    International Verification of Neural Networks Competition
    (VNN-COMP)~\cite{vnncomp} across multiple years.  The
    auto\_LiRPA~\cite{auto-lirpa} library generalises these
    techniques to arbitrary architectures and supports GPU-accelerated
    batch verification.

    Most verification work targets classification or regression
    networks in isolation: given an input region (typically an
    $\ell_p$-ball), does the network maintain its prediction?  This
    thesis extends the verification target to multi-round closed-loop
    control, where the network's output feeds back into its future
    input through an environment model---a setting that requires
    composing verification across two networks over multiple time
    steps.


    % ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
    \subsection{Verification of Neural Network-Based Systems}
    \label{sec:related-nn4sys}

    A growing line of work applies neural network verification to
    \emph{systems} settings where the network operates as a component
    within a larger pipeline.  Wu et al.~\cite{splitting} verify
    properties of GNN-based job schedulers, addressing challenges of
    deeper networks and richer specifications (strategy-proofness,
    sharing incentive) than those encountered in image classification.
    SpecTRA~\cite{spec-tra} mines pre-/post-condition specifications
    from system traces, enabling verification of neural components
    without externally imposed correctness criteria.
    ArjomandBigdeli et al.~\cite{nn4sys-verification} verify safety of
    neural network control systems in continuous time.  They abstract
    the controller into piecewise linear models with bounded error,
    verify the abstraction with $\alpha$-$\beta$-CROWN, and compose it
    with CORA reachability analysis over the plant dynamics to check
    closed-loop safety properties.

    A common limitation across this body of work is the treatment of
    the \emph{environment}.  Existing approaches either verify the
    neural component in isolation---ignoring feedback
    dynamics---or assume a known, symbolic environment model.  In
    closed-loop systems like ABR streaming, the controller's action
    alters the state it will observe next, so single-step verification
    provides incomplete coverage.  This thesis addresses this gap by
    constructing a statistically certified neural environment model and
    composing it with the controller verification over multiple rounds.


    % ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
    \subsection{Adversarial Robustness in Control Systems}
    \label{sec:related-adversarial}

    Adversarial vulnerability of neural networks was first
    demonstrated in image classification~\cite{attack}, with the Fast
    Gradient Sign Method (FGSM)~\cite{goodfellow-fgsm} and Projected
    Gradient Descent (PGD)~\cite{pgd} becoming standard attack
    algorithms.  Huang et al.~\cite{huang-adversarial-rl} extended
    these techniques to deep RL, showing that policies trained on Atari
    games are vulnerable to small observation perturbations that cause
    catastrophic performance drops.  Gleave et
    al.~\cite{gleave-adversarial-policies} demonstrated that
    adversarial \emph{policies}---agents trained to exploit an
    opponent's learned policy---can defeat RL agents in multi-agent
    settings even without directly perturbing observations.

    On the defence side, randomized smoothing~\cite{randomized-smoothing}
    provides probabilistic certified robustness guarantees by
    averaging predictions over Gaussian-perturbed inputs, but applies
    to classifiers rather than sequential control policies.
    Certified training methods that incorporate verification bounds
    into the training loss have shown promise for small networks but
    do not scale to the multi-step, closed-loop setting considered
    here.

    The ABR streaming setting differs from prior adversarial robustness
    work in two respects.  First, input features are \emph{physically
    coupled}: throughput is definitionally chunk size divided by
    download time, so perturbations must respect this constraint to
    remain realisable.  Second, the adversary's capabilities are
    constrained by the network stack rather than arbitrary
    $\ell_p$-balls: perturbations must correspond to plausible network
    conditions.  The specification-guided PGD attack in
    \ref{chap:attack} addresses both constraints by restricting
    perturbations to SpecTRA-derived input regions.


    % ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
    \subsection{Adaptive Bitrate Streaming}
    \label{sec:related-abr}

    ABR algorithms have evolved through three generations.
    Buffer-based approaches such as BOLA~\cite{bola} select bitrates
    based on current buffer occupancy, offering simplicity and
    theoretical optimality guarantees under idealised conditions.
    Model-predictive control approaches such as
    MPC~\cite{mpc} use throughput forecasts to plan bitrate
    trajectories over a short horizon.  Oboe~\cite{oboe} auto-tunes
    ABR algorithm parameters to match detected network conditions,
    bridging rule-based and adaptive approaches.

    Learning-based controllers represent the current frontier.
    Pensieve~\cite{pensieve} trains a neural network via reinforcement
    learning to maximise a composite QoE objective, achieving
    $10$--$70\%$ gains over rule-based baselines.
    Fugu~\cite{fugu} combines a supervised throughput predictor with
    model-predictive control, demonstrating that hybrid
    learned--algorithmic designs can match or exceed pure-RL
    approaches in live deployment.

    Despite these QoE gains, the robustness properties of learning-based
    controllers have received little attention.  Pensieve was evaluated
    under simulated and real network traces but not under adversarial
    conditions or formal analysis.  Fugu's MPC component is
    interpretable and can be analysed independently, but its learned
    throughput predictor remains susceptible to input perturbations.
    To our knowledge, this thesis provides the first formal robustness
    analysis of a neural ABR controller, encompassing both adversarial
    vulnerability assessment and exhaustive reachability verification.


    % ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
    \subsection{Formal Methods for Networked Systems}
    \label{sec:related-formal-net}

    Formal verification has a long history in networked systems.
    Header Space Analysis~\cite{hsa} introduced geometric reasoning
    about packet transformations, enabling static reachability checking
    across network configurations.  Subsequent tools extended these
    ideas to richer property classes including loop-freedom, isolation,
    and equivalence checking.

    These methods operate on symbolic, rule-based systems where the
    forwarding logic is encoded in match-action tables with finite
    state spaces.  The introduction of neural components into network
    systems---the NN4Sys paradigm---disrupts this tradition: neural
    networks encode continuous, nonlinear decision boundaries that
    resist the finite-state abstractions on which classical network
    verification relies.  Bridging these two worlds requires combining
    neural network verification techniques (such as LiRPA) with
    domain-specific modelling of the system environment, as developed
    in this thesis.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{chap:conclusion}

    This thesis developed the first end-to-end framework for formally
    reasoning about the closed-loop QoE behaviour of neurosymbolic ABR
    controllers, addressing the three challenges identified in
    Section~\ref{subsec:problem}: multi-round bound propagation,
    unknown environment dynamics, and physical coupling of input
    features.

    A specification-guided PGD attack on Pensieve found $581$
    adversarial examples across $30{,}000$ states, reducing bitrate by
    $35$--$65\%$ with a perturbation budget of just $10\%$ on a single
    feature (\ref{chap:attack}).  A statistically certified
    environment predictor achieved $\geq 98.46\%$ accuracy at $90\%$
    confidence, providing the missing symbolic component for
    closed-loop analysis (\ref{chap:env}).  Building on these, we
    designed an end-to-end verification pipeline that alternates
    between Pensieve-phase bound propagation and environment-phase
    interval propagation to build hierarchical reachability trees with
    $\Delta$QoE annotations over multiple control rounds, with formal
    soundness guarantees and a statistical compounding bound of
    $p^5 \geq 0.9246$ (\ref{chap:verify}).

    The pipeline provides a general methodology for formally reasoning
    about worst-case multi-round behaviour of neural controllers in
    closed-loop networked systems.  By enumerating all verified action
    sequences and their $\Delta$QoE within each specification region,
    the reachability tree provides exhaustive coverage that complements
    the targeted but incomplete coverage of adversarial attacks.
    
    Looking ahead, the reachability tree itself could drive targeted
    testing: action sequences with the worst $\Delta$QoE expose
    concrete low-QoE trajectories that can be mined at scale and fed
    back into training as hard negatives, closing the loop toward
    verification-guided retraining of ABR controllers that are
    verifiably safe by construction. As neural controllers
    increasingly replace hand-crafted heuristics in networked systems,
    certified environment modelling and compositional reachability
    analysis offer a principled path toward deploying these systems
    with formal guarantees.
